# Mastering the What–When–Where Processing System

## What “Processing” Really Means
Processing is the act of **deciding**, not doing.

Every time you process an item, you answer three questions:
1. **What** is the very next action?
2. **When** will that action be done?
3. **Where** does this item belong when processed?

This three‑question habit is the core of effective time management.

---

## The Golden Rule of Processing
Process **one item at a time**.

If you touch two items at once—or switch away mid‑decision—you’re switchtasking, which wastes time and attention.  
Pick one item from one approved gathering point and finish deciding before touching the next.

To build the habit, say the questions **out loud**:
- “What is the next step?”
- “When will it be done?”
- “Where is its home?”

Focused processing saves hours every week.

---

# Processing Question 1: What Is the Next Step?

## Define the Single Next Action
Your task is not to solve everything—only to identify the **very next action** that moves the item forward.

Even large projects have a next step.

If you feel tempted to skip an item because it’s vague or uncomfortable, pause and stay with it a few seconds longer. That’s often where progress happens.

**Good enough is sufficient.** A reasonable guess beats perfect hesitation.

---

## Decide If It Should Be Delegated—or Dropped
While identifying the next step, also ask:
- Would someone else be better suited to do this?
- Is this still aligned with my priorities and values?

If you delegate:
- Create a *waiting‑for* reminder to follow up later

If the task no longer matters:
- Disengage politely and move on

Clarity here prevents unnecessary busyness.

---

# Processing Question 2: When Will It Be Done?

## Be Specific
Avoid vague timing like “sometime next week.”  
Choose a **specific date and time** instead.

Use these rules:

---

### Rule 1: Do It Now (≤ 5 Minutes)
If the action takes five minutes or less, **do it immediately**.  
Delaying short tasks costs more time than it saves.

---

### Rule 2: Put It on the Calendar (> 15 Minutes or Time‑Sensitive)
If an action:
- Takes more than 15 minutes, **or**
- Has a real deadline

…it belongs on your calendar as a firm commitment.

Calendaring breaks the habit of indefinite postponement.

---

### Rule 3: Use Task Reminders (5–15 Minutes, Flexible)
Short, non‑urgent tasks work well as **task reminders**.

These are:
- Flexible
- Just‑in‑time prompts
- Easy to reschedule without guilt

Calendar = solid commitments  
Task reminders = flexible options

---

## Additional Timing Tips
- **Batch recurring tasks** (e.g., bill paying) to reduce switching
- **Schedule buffer time**, roughly 10 minutes per hour of planned work
- **Do high‑value work sooner**, delay low‑value work when reasonable

Time choice is how priorities become real.

---

# Processing Question 3: Where Is Its Home?

## Everything Has a Home
Adopt this rule:
> **Everything has a home. No visitors allowed.**

Items of similar type belong together—in folders, trays, shelves, or digital directories. This makes finding and returning items fast.

Keep homes no more than **¾ full** so putting things away is easy.

---

## Physical vs. Digital Storage
- **Physical**: When in doubt, discard (especially paper)
- **Digital**: When in doubt, keep—storage is cheap and searchable

Store contacts (numbers, addresses) in one synced system so they’re always accessible.

---

## Optional: Create “Virtual Homes” for Tasks
For recurring contexts, group tasks by situation:
- “1:1 – Nicole”
- “Team Meeting”
- “Grocery Store”

These virtual homes free your mind from remembering details and improve follow‑through.

# Paper and Paperless Filing—Made Simple

## A Hybrid Filing Approach
Many environments need both paper and digital.

### Non‑Financial Paper
- Use an **alphabetical filing system** with hanging folders
- Place papers into labeled manila folders
- Use one set per category if volume is high

### Go Paperless When Possible
- Scan or photograph documents using a notes app
- Text becomes searchable
- Physical paper can be recycled after processing

Handwritten notes can be digitized the same way.

---

## Financial Documents
- Create **12 folders**, one per month
- Add receipts/contracts as they are processed
- Start a new set each year

This keeps financial records simple and complete.

---

# Digital Storage—Six Universal Principles

1. Create top‑level folders: **Work** and **Personal**
2. Inside each, create **broad categories**
3. Add **one more layer** of meaningful subfolders (no more)
4. Keep an **“Old”** folder in subfolders for previous versions
5. Save new versions with dates in filenames (e.g., YYYY‑MM‑DD)
6. Use **automatic cloud backups** for everything

Three layers is enough—search does the rest.

# Processing Your First Inbox

## Practice with Real Items
Start with your **one‑week inbox**.

For each item:
1. Ask **What?**
2. Decide **When?**
3. Assign **Where?**

Repeat—even for obvious items. Repetition builds skill.

Examples:
- Junk mail → shred now → shred tray
- Statement → review now → file by month
- Project note → schedule time → recycle note

---

### Your Turn
Pause now and process items **one at a time** for about **one hour**.
Then move to larger inboxes if time allows.

---

# Processing Your Notepad (Paper or Digital)

## Adjusting the “Where” Step
Notes usually contain both information and actions.

- Mark actions clearly (e.g., checkboxes)
- Process each action with What–When–Where
- File remaining notes as reference

Use the same three‑layer structure (work → category → subject).

Paper notes can be filed or scanned after actions are processed.

Practice this for **30 minutes** to build fluency.

---

# Setting Your Processing Schedule

## Make Processing a Weekly Habit
Aim to empty each approved gathering point **at least once per week** (not all at once).

Recommended baseline:
- **5 hours per week**, in focused blocks
- Blocks of **≥ 1 hour** are most effective (momentum builds after ~25 minutes)

Choose low‑interruption times (e.g., early mornings).

Test a schedule, then refine.

---

## Handling the Backlog (One‑Time Effort)
Estimate extra time needed:
- ~1 hour per large box
- ~1 hour per 30 unresolved tasks from mind clearing

Don’t do it all at once.
Schedule extra processing over the **next month**.

Reaching zero is deeply freeing—and worth the effort.

---

## Reflection Questions
- Am I processing or jumping into action?
- Do I answer all three questions every time?
- Is my processing time protected?
- Have I scheduled my backlog time?

---

## Key Takeaway
Productivity improves when decisions are made deliberately and in sequence.

By mastering:
- **What** to do
- **When** to do it
- **Where** it belongs

…you replace stress and chaos with clarity and control—one item at a time.
