# Applying What–When–Where Processing to Email

## Email Is Just Another Gathering Point
Email often feels special — and overwhelming — but it follows the **same processing system** as your physical inbox.

For each email, ask:
1. **What** is the next action?
2. **When** will that action happen?
3. **Where** does this email belong once processed?

Your long‑term goal is to bring your inbox to **zero** — at least once per week.

---

## Why the Inbox Becomes Overwhelming
Inbox overload usually comes from habits such as:
- Using the inbox as a reminder list
- Leaving messages unread as a signal of “still to do”
- Keeping emails visible out of fear of losing them

These habits create constant attention switching, which increases stress and reduces accuracy.

Instead of focusing on unread messages, focus on the **total number** of emails in your inbox — that’s the real indicator of workload.

---

# Using an Email Archive Folder

## Stop Filing — Start Archiving
Detailed email folder systems are time‑consuming and unnecessary.

Modern email search is powerful, so a much simpler rule works better:
> **Processed email goes to Archive.**

Archiving:
- Removes email from view
- Keeps it fully searchable
- Reduces visual clutter and switching cost

Many email apps already include an archive feature.  
If not, create a single folder called **Archive**.

---

## Trust Search, Not Memory
Before relying on archive:
- Learn how your email search works
- Practice searching by sender, keyword, or date

This is faster and more reliable than manual filing.

---

# Creating Email Rules or Filters

## Automate the Processing Where Possible
Email filters (also called rules) automatically answer the what–when–where questions for certain messages.

Good candidates for filters:
- Coupons
- Newsletters
- Routine notifications
- Informational updates

For example:
- If an email is from a specific sender
- Then skip the inbox and archive it
- Optionally tag or label it for reference

This keeps low‑value emails from ever entering your inbox.

---

## Use Filters Carefully
Avoid filters for messages that:
- Might require action in the future
- Could change importance over time

Used incorrectly, filters can create **hidden gathering points**.

Used correctly, they save enormous processing time.

---

# Processing Example Emails

## Example 1: Unwanted Newsletter
- **What:** Unsubscribe  
- **When:** Now (takes < 5 minutes)  
- **Where:** Delete  

Done immediately and removed permanently.

---

## Example 2: Scheduling Request
- **What:** Check calendar → reply with availability  
- **When:** Now  
- **Where:** Archive after responding  

If waiting for a reply:
- Create a follow‑up reminder, or
- Snooze the sent email until a specific date

---

## Example 3: Task That Requires Time
- **What:** Review supplier’s website  
- **When:** Schedule 30 minutes on calendar  
- **Where:** Archive email  

Tasks taking more than 15 minutes or needing focus belong on the calendar.

---

### Your Turn
Pause and process your email **one message at a time** for about **one hour**.  
The repetition is what makes this automatic.

---

# Processing Email vs. Checking Email

## These Are Not the Same Thing
**Processing email** = deciding what, when, where  
**Checking email** = answering one question:
> *Can this wait until my next processing time?*

Checking should be short and intentional — not continuous.

---

## How to Check Email Properly
During a brief check:
- Scan incoming messages
- Ask “Can this wait?”
- If yes → leave it (flag or snooze if helpful)
- If no → process it immediately

This approach allows urgent issues to be handled quickly without processing everything on the spot.

---

## Choose a Checking Schedule
Select a rhythm that fits your role, such as:
- Every hour
- A few set times per day

Limit each check to **5–15 minutes**.

Even reducing checks to once per hour dramatically lowers switching costs.

---

# Reducing a Large Inbox (Catch‑Up Phase)

## Step 1: Archive Older Emails
If your inbox contains hundreds or thousands of messages:
1. Pick a reasonable cutoff date (e.g., two months ago)
2. Search for all emails older than that date
3. Select all → Archive

This instantly makes your inbox manageable.

---

## Step 2: Schedule Catch‑Up Processing
Plan extra processing time:
- About **1 hour per 100 emails**

Spread this over the next month rather than forcing it all at once.

Progress matters more than speed.

---

## Email Is Conquered Through Consistency
Bringing your inbox to zero is not a one‑time victory — it’s a **weekly outcome** of:
- Focused processing
- Smart filters
- Intentional checking
- Trusting archive + search

When you control email, email stops controlling you.

---

## Reflection Questions
- Am I processing email or just reacting to it?
- How often do I check messages?
- Do I use my inbox as a reminder?
- What would change if my inbox hit zero weekly?

---

## Key Takeaway
Email becomes manageable when it is treated like any other task stream.

By:
- Applying what–when–where consistently
- Archiving instead of organizing
- Automating low‑value messages
- Separating checking from processing

You dramatically reduce attention switching, reclaim time, and restore mental focus.
