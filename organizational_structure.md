## Overview

An organization’s **structure** has a significant impact on how projects are:
- Planned
- Managed
- Executed
- Supported

Organizational structure determines:
- How much authority a project manager has
- How easily resources can be assigned
- How likely projects are to succeed

Common organizational structures fall into three main categories:
1. Functional
2. Matrix
3. Projectized

---

## 1. Functional Organization

A **functional organization** follows a traditional hierarchy.

### Key Characteristics
- Each employee reports to **one functional manager**
- Departments are organized by specialty (e.g., IT, Finance, HR)
- Projects are not the primary focus

### Impact on Projects
- Projects have **low priority**
- Project managers have **little to no authority**
- Functional managers typically control:
  - Budgets
  - Resource assignments
- Resources report to functional managers, not the project manager
- Project staff, including project managers, often:
  - Split time between project work and operational duties

### Result
- Difficult environment for projects
- Slow decision-making
- High risk of delays and resource conflicts

---

## 2. Matrix Organization

Matrix organizations are still based on functional departments but provide **greater support for projects**.

Resources report to **two managers**:
- A functional manager
- A project manager

### Types of Matrix Structures
- **Weak Matrix**
- **Balanced Matrix**
- **Strong Matrix**

The difference lies in how much authority is given to the project manager.

---

### Weak Matrix

- Very similar to a functional organization
- Project manager has limited authority
- Functional manager retains most control

---

### Balanced Matrix

- Authority is shared between functional and project managers
- Project manager can make some decisions
- Resources split time between projects and functional work

---

### Strong Matrix

- Greater emphasis on projects
- Project managers have significant authority
- Project managers and project administrative staff:
  - Often work full time on projects
- Resources are more clearly aligned with project goals

---

## 3. Projectized Organization

A **projectized organization** is centered almost entirely around projects.

### Key Characteristics
- Most people work on projects full time
- Resources report directly to the project manager
- Project managers have:
  - High authority
  - Control over budgets
  - Control over resources

### Impact on Projects
- Clear accountability
- Faster decision-making
- Strong alignment with project objectives
- Easier to achieve project results

### Result
- Highly supportive environment for project success

---

## Comparison Summary

| Structure     | PM Authority | Resource Availability | Project Priority |
|---------------|--------------|-----------------------|------------------|
| Functional    | Very Low     | Very Limited          | Low              |
| Weak Matrix   | Low          | Limited               | Medium‑Low       |
| Balanced Matrix | Medium     | Shared                | Medium           |
| Strong Matrix | High         | Mostly Dedicated      | High             |
| Projectized   | Very High    | Fully Dedicated       | Very High        |

---

## Why Organizational Structure Matters

Organizational structure influences:
- How projects are executed
- How conflicts are resolved
- How empowered a project manager can be
- How easily projects receive support

Understanding these characteristics helps project managers:
- Set realistic expectations
- Navigate authority limitations
- Adapt leadership style to the environment

---

## Key Takeaway

There is no single “best” structure for every organization.  
Success depends on understanding:
- The structure you work within
- Its strengths and limitations
- How to manage projects effectively inside that structure

Use these characteristics to determine which structure your organization uses and how it affects your projects.

## What Is Organizational Culture?

Organizational culture is the collection of factors that guide how people:
- Behave
- Make decisions
- Interact with one another

Culture is shaped by shared:
- Values
- Beliefs
- Assumptions
- Habits
- Language

These elements strongly influence how projects are managed and executed.

---

## Mission and Vision

An organization’s **mission and vision** form the foundation of its culture.

### Impact on Projects
- Projects aligned with the mission are more likely to:
  - Receive attention
  - Secure resources
  - Gain leadership support
- When faced with difficult decisions:
  - The mission can guide what the “right” choice is

Projects that clearly support organizational goals tend to be more successful.

---

## Leadership and Authority

Leadership style is a major cultural factor.

### Empowering Cultures
- Management defines clear goals
- Responsibility is delegated to employees
- Trust is placed in teams

In these environments:
- Project managers are empowered
- Decision‑making is faster
- Accountability is clearer

---

### Centralized Authority Cultures

- Authority is rarely delegated
- Management approval is required for many decisions

In these organizations:
- Project managers must work closely with leadership
- Building trust is essential
- Progress may depend on stakeholder relationships

---

## Work Environment

The work environment reflects organizational culture.

### Positive Work Environment
- Employees are motivated
- Collaboration is encouraged
- Lessons learned are openly shared
- Continuous improvement is valued

Managing projects is easier because:
- Team members contribute ideas
- People are accustomed to providing feedback

---

### Negative Work Environment
- Low morale
- Resistance to feedback
- Limited openness

In these situations:
- Project managers spend more time managing behavior
- Communication and motivation require extra effort

---

## Rules vs. Innovation

Organizations differ in how strictly they expect rules to be followed.

### Rules‑Driven Cultures
- Emphasize procedures and compliance
- Expect adherence even if objectives are harder to achieve

Breaking rules may be possible, but requires:
- Knowing which rules are flexible
- Planning for risks if alternative approaches fail

---

### Innovation‑Driven Cultures
- Encourage experimentation
- Expect employees to question past practices
- Value creative problem solving

In these environments:
- Projects can adapt more easily
- Non‑standard solutions are more acceptable

---

## Results vs. Procedures

An important cultural question is:
- Are **results** more important than **procedures**, or the other way around?

Examples:
- Is it acceptable to follow all rules even if objectives are not met?
- Or is achieving results the priority, even if procedures must be adapted?

Understanding these boundaries helps project managers:
- Make better decisions
- Avoid cultural conflicts
- Deliver results without violating expectations

---

## Change Management and Culture

Organizational attitude toward risk affects change management.

### Risk‑Averse Cultures
- Prefer stability and predictability
- Change requires:
  - Multiple reviews
  - Several approval levels

Change processes are often:
- Formal
- Slower
- Highly controlled

---

### Change‑Friendly Cultures
- View change as normal
- Accept uncertainty
- Simplify approval processes

Projects can:
- Adjust more quickly
- Respond faster to new information

---

## Cultural Differences in Project Teams

With distributed and global teams, **cultural diversity** becomes critical.

Team members may:
- Communicate differently
- Respond to conflict differently
- Interpret behavior through cultural norms

**Example:**
- In some cultures, not showing weakness is valued
- In others, that same behavior may be seen as arrogance

Project managers must:
- Recognize cultural differences
- Adapt communication styles
- Promote mutual understanding

---

## Key Takeaway

Organizational culture strongly influences:
- Decision‑making
- Authority
- Communication
- Change management
- Project success

To increase your chances of success:
- Understand your organization’s culture
- Respect cultural boundaries
- Adapt project management practices accordingly

Effective project managers manage **people, processes, and culture together**.

## Overview

Project management software can significantly **reduce manual effort**, improve **visibility**, and enable **collaboration** across project teams.

Different types of software support different aspects of project management, and no single tool fits every project or organization.

---

## Scheduling Software

Scheduling software helps build, manage, and track project schedules.

### Common Scheduling Tools
Examples include:
- Microsoft Project
- Oracle Primavera
- LiquidPlanner
- Jira
- Smartsheet
- Wrike
- Asana

### Waterfall vs Agile Support
- **Waterfall‑focused tools**
  - Microsoft Project
  - Oracle Primavera  
  These tools support Agile but are not optimized for it.

- **Agile‑focused tools**
  - Jira  
  Jira is primarily designed for Agile, but it can be adapted for Waterfall projects.

When selecting a tool:
- Ensure it supports the **project management approach** your organization uses
- Verify compatibility with both Waterfall and Agile if needed

Many modern scheduling tools are **cloud‑based**, making it easier for teams to collaborate in real time.

---

## Word Processing Software

Project managers create many documents throughout a project, such as:
- Project charters
- Requirements documents
- Status reports
- Lessons learned

A word processing tool is essential.

### Best Practice
- Create **document templates**
- Reuse standard layouts across projects
- Save time and maintain consistency

---

## Spreadsheet Software

Spreadsheets are a core tool for analysis and calculations.

They are commonly used for:
- Budgets and cost tracking
- Financial analysis
- Risk analysis and prioritization
- Data comparison and what‑if scenarios

Spreadsheets are especially useful when:
- Custom calculations are required
- Data needs to be sorted or filtered quickly

---

## Presentation Software

Presentation tools are useful for **high‑level communication**.

Examples include:
- PowerPoint
- Prezi

Typical use cases:
- Executive briefings
- Stakeholder updates
- Combining visuals, charts, and summarized data
- Communicating complex information simply

---

## Collaboration Software

Because projects involve multiple people, collaboration tools are critical.

### Common Collaboration Tools
- Basecamp
- Asana
- Microsoft SharePoint

These tools help teams:
- Share and manage documents
- Track issues and action items
- Communicate asynchronously
- Manage basic workflows

Cloud‑based collaboration tools support:
- Distributed teams
- Remote work
- Real‑time updates

---

## Enterprise Project Management (EPM) Software

Organizations managing **many large or complex projects** may need enterprise‑level tools.

### Key Capabilities
Enterprise project management software can:
- Identify available resources based on skills
- Show resource availability over time
- Track risks and issues across projects
- Maintain centralized document libraries
- Provide portfolio‑level visibility

EPM tools are useful when:
- Multiple projects compete for shared resources
- Standardization across projects is required
- Leadership needs consolidated reporting

---

## How to Choose the Right Software

When evaluating project management software, consider:

- Organizational culture and work environment
- Software budget
- Project management methodologies in use
- Number of projects being managed
- Project size and complexity

The best tool is the one that:
- Fits how your organization works
- Supports your project approach
- Scales with your project portfolio

---

## Key Takeaway

Project management software is a support tool—not a replacement for good project management.

Choosing the right combination of tools can:
- Improve efficiency
- Enhance collaboration
- Increase visibility
- Support better decision‑making throughout the project lifecycle

## Overview

Online project management software products vary widely in **capability, complexity, and cost**.  
Although many products are labeled as “project management tools,” they can generally be grouped into **three levels of capability**:

1. Mostly collaborative tools  
2. Mid‑level project management tools  
3. Robust project management tools  

Understanding these categories helps you select software that fits your organization’s needs without unnecessary complexity.

---

## Levels of Capability

---

## 1. Mostly Collaborative Tools

Mostly collaborative tools focus on **communication and teamwork**.  
They are:
- Easy to set up
- Reasonably priced
- Well suited for Agile teams
- Limited in traditional project management features

### Common Capabilities
Mostly collaborative tools usually provide:
- Communication and collaboration features  
  - Messaging  
  - Message boards  
  - Notifications  
  - Basic workflows
- Work management
- Tasks, subtasks, and milestones
- Sometimes basic dependencies and start/due dates
- Templates
- Customization and automation
- Integration with other software
- Support for Agile project management

### Common Limitations
These tools often lack:
- Robust scheduling and dependency management
- Automatic date recalculation
- Resource management (e.g., overallocation handling)
- Cost management (labor, materials, budgets)
- Risk management

### Popular Mostly Collaborative Tools
- Asana  
- Trello  
- ClickUp  
- Basecamp  

---

## 2. Mid‑Level Project Management Tools

Mid‑level tools strike a balance between **collaboration and structured project management**.

They are:
- Relatively easy to implement
- Reasonably priced for their capabilities
- Suitable for both Agile and Waterfall projects

### Common Capabilities
Mid‑level tools usually offer:
- Communication and collaboration features
- Multiple project views, such as:
  - Task lists
  - Gantt charts
  - Kanban boards
  - Progress views
  - Workload views
- Tasks, subtasks, and milestones
- Task dependencies and task dates
- Sometimes automatic date recalculation
- Sometimes critical path calculation
- Baselines
- Sometimes cost management and budgets
- Templates
- Customization and automation
- Integration with other software

### Common Limitations
Mid‑level tools often do not include:
- Fully robust scheduling
- Advanced cost management
- Formal risk management capabilities

### Popular Mid‑Level Tools
- Smartsheet  
- Monday.com  
- Wrike  

---

## 3. Robust Project Management Tools

Robust tools offer **advanced capabilities** designed for complex projects and large organizations.

### Advanced Capabilities
Robust tools typically provide:
- Robust scheduling
- Automated workload and resource management
- Comprehensive cost management, including:
  - Hourly rates
  - Multiple rates per person
  - Billable and non‑billable time tracking
- Portfolio and program management
- Risk management

---

### Trade‑Offs of Robust Tools

While powerful, robust tools come with challenges:
- Complex configuration and setup
- May require consulting support
- Ongoing management by experienced administrators
- Significant training requirements for users
- Higher costs than other categories

---

### Popular Robust Project Management Tools
- Teamwork  
- Zoho Projects  
- Jira (Agile only)  
- Microsoft Project Online  
- Oracle Primavera  

---

## Choosing the Right Tool

Project management software varies widely in:
- Approach
- Features
- Strengths and weaknesses
- Pricing models

When selecting a tool, you must evaluate whether it:
- Aligns with your project management methodologies
- Supports your organizational culture
- Matches your project complexity
- Fits your budget
- Scales with your number of projects

The **best tool** is not necessarily the most powerful—it is the one that best supports how your organization works.

---

## Key Takeaway

Online project management tools range from **simple collaboration platforms** to **enterprise‑grade systems**.

By understanding capability levels, project managers can:
- Avoid underpowered tools
- Prevent unnecessary complexity
- Select software that enables project success

For deeper comparisons and evaluations, further study of specific tools and their capabilities is recommended.
