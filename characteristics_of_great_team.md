# Use the Johari Window to Understand Yourself

## Why Openness Matters in Teams
High‑performing teams rely on honest, open communication. One widely used way to understand openness is the **Johari Window**, a simple model that helps explain how self‑awareness and transparency affect teamwork.

The model was developed by two psychologists whose names combine to form “Johari.” Its purpose is not assessment, but **reflection and growth**.

---

## The Two Dimensions of the Johari Window
The Johari Window looks at two questions:

- How much do *you* understand about yourself?
- How much of that is visible to *others*?

These dimensions intersect to show how openly you operate within a team.

---

## How Well Do You Really Know Yourself?
At first, it’s tempting to assume we understand ourselves completely — after all, we live inside our own thoughts.

But test that assumption:
- Try describing yourself using just three words.

Most people find this harder than expected.

Why? Because:
- You haven’t experienced every possible situation
- You don’t yet know how you’ll react to unknown challenges
- Your future capabilities haven’t been revealed

Self‑knowledge is incomplete by nature — and that’s normal.

---

## How Much of the Real You Do Others See?
Now consider the second dimension: what your colleagues actually know about you.

Ask yourself:
- Would others describe you using the same three words?
- What percentage of the “real you” is visible at work?

People’s answers vary widely — from very little to almost everything.

The important question isn’t *what percentage*, but **whether the level of openness supports team success**.

---

## Why Excessive Privacy Can Hurt Teams
When too much of who you are stays hidden, you create what is often called a **facade** — the part of you that others don’t see.

Large facades cause problems:
- Others sense something is being withheld
- Trust becomes harder to build
- Mutual openness decreases

People often respond to guardedness by becoming guarded themselves. This quietly erodes teamwork.

---

## Strengths and Weaknesses Need to Be Known
For a team to perform well:
- Strengths must be visible so they can be used
- Weaknesses must be visible so they can be supported or compensated for

When your capabilities are hidden:
- You reduce your own effectiveness
- You prevent the team from working around your limitations

Openness is not oversharing — it is enabling collaboration.

---

## The Fear Behind Facades
Many people hide parts of themselves because they fear:
- Being judged
- Being disliked
- Being exposed

In reality, this fear is usually misplaced.

People tend to trust:
- Authenticity more than performance
- Consistency more than polish

Ironically, others often distrust the mask more than the real person behind it.

---

## Why Authenticity Increases Performance
You cannot add abilities you don’t have — but you *can* remove effectiveness by hiding who you are.

Covering up parts of yourself:
- Reduces confidence
- Limits contribution
- Makes work feel harder than it needs to be

Being yourself allows your full capability to show up at work.

---

## Expanding the “Open” Area
The most effective teams have large “open” areas:
- People understand themselves reasonably well
- Others understand them too

This increases:
- Trust
- Predictability
- Mutual support

Expanding this area takes courage — but the payoff is significant.

---

## Reflection Questions
Ask yourself:
- How much of myself do I show at work?
- What am I hiding, and why?
- Would being more open help the team?
- Would it help *me* perform better?

---

## Key Takeaway
You don’t need to be perfect to be effective — but you do need to be real.

Reducing your facade and allowing others to see the genuine you:
- Builds trust
- Improves collaboration
- Allows the team to work with your strengths, not around your opacity

The Johari Window reminds us that openness is not a personality trait — it is a **choice**.

# Get Feedback to Be a Better Team Member

## Why Feedback Matters
Self‑awareness is one of the most important factors in personal and professional success. Without it, you may:
- Under‑use your strengths
- Be repeatedly tripped up by weaknesses you don’t see
- Unintentionally frustrate others in your team

A powerful way to increase self‑awareness is to **actively seek feedback**.

---

## The Johari Window: A Quick Reminder
The Johari Window helps explain self‑knowledge through four areas:
- What you know about yourself
- What others know about you
- What is hidden from others (your *facade*)
- What is hidden from you but visible to others (your *blind spot*)

Previously, we looked at reducing the facade. Now the focus is on **shrinking the blind spot**.

---

## Understanding Your Blind Spot
Your blind spot contains traits that **others notice but you don’t**.

These can be:
- Negative (e.g. seeming unclear, controlling, impatient)
- Positive (e.g. being seen as insightful, calm, or creative without realizing it)

Both matter. You need to know about weaknesses so you can adjust — and about strengths so you can use them intentionally.

---

## Why We Avoid Feedback
Most people hesistate to ask for feedback because:
- They fear criticism
- They worry about being judged
- They imagine the truth is worse than it really is

In reality, the fear is usually far worse than the feedback itself. Learning what’s in your blind spot often feels **relieving and empowering**, not damaging.

---

## Courage Expands the “Arena”
When you:
- Share more of yourself openly (reduce the facade)
- Seek honest feedback (reduce the blind spot)

You enlarge the **arena** — the part of you that is understood by both you and your colleagues.

This shared understanding is the foundation of strong teamwork.

Examples:
- If you lose patience under pressure, the team can help buffer that
- If you shine at idea generation in difficult situations, the team can draw on it deliberately
- If you struggle with detail, others can compensate early

Unknown traits can’t be managed; known ones can.

---

## Feedback Unlocks Potential
Beyond known strengths and weaknesses lies something else: **unexplored potential**.

These are abilities that:
- You haven’t discovered yet
- Others haven’t seen yet

Growth often begins when feedback nudges you toward something you hadn’t considered possible.

---

## How to Ask for Feedback Effectively
To uncover your blind spot:
- Choose people who work closely with you
- Ask open, specific questions like:
  - “What should I do more of?”
  - “What sometimes gets in the way when we work together?”
  - “Is there anything I could change that would make collaboration easier?”

Listen carefully — especially to subtle clues.

---

## Learn to Read Between the Lines
People rarely give blunt criticism.

Examples:
- “You could explain your ideas a bit more clearly”
  → Communication may be unclear
- “It would be nice to have more freedom in how I do my tasks”
  → You may be too controlling

These hints are valuable signals.

If someone *is* direct, treat that honesty as a gift — not an attack.

---

## Don’t Reject the Message
When criticized, it’s tempting to:
- Dismiss the feedback
- Judge the person giving it
- Defend yourself immediately

Try instead to:
- Pause
- Thank them
- Reflect before responding

Even imperfect feedback usually contains something useful.

---

## Make a Commitment to Ask
Becoming a better team member requires bravery:
- The bravery to be open
- The bravery to ask
- The bravery to change

Promise yourself to seek feedback regularly and thoughtfully. It’s one of the most effective ways to grow — for yourself and for your team.

---

## Key Takeaway
You already know some things about yourself.  
Others see things you don’t.

When you invite feedback:
- Self‑knowledge increases
- Trust deepens
- Team effectiveness improves

Growth begins the moment you decide to ask.

# Learn Three Simple Ways to Be Nice on a Team

## Why Being Nice Is a Team Skill
Being a strong team player isn’t only about competence or effort — it’s also about **supporting the people you work with**.

Some people naturally focus on relationships, while others (often under pressure) concentrate mainly on tasks. But team support shouldn’t be left to just a few “people‑focused” individuals. Everyone can contribute.

The good news: being supportive doesn’t require huge effort. A few small actions can make a noticeable difference.

---

## Three High‑Impact Behaviours of Great Team Players
When you observe highly liked and effective team members, three simple behaviours appear again and again:

1. **Thanking people**
2. **Complimenting people**
3. **Offering help**

These are small, everyday actions — but they have a powerful effect on morale and trust.

---

## 1. Thank People
Saying “thank you” costs nothing, takes seconds, and has lasting impact.

Many people go weeks or even months without being thanked for their work. That lack of recognition quietly drains motivation.

Thanking someone:
- Acknowledges effort
- Builds goodwill
- Reinforces positive behaviour

It’s basic courtesy — and surprisingly rare.

---

## 2. Compliment Good Work
Compliments are different from thanks.

You can compliment someone:
- For a strength they have
- For consistently good work
- For how they contribute to the team — not just you personally

This type of recognition:
- Boosts confidence
- Helps people see the value they bring
- Encourages them to keep using their strengths

Be specific and genuine — vague praise feels forgettable.

---

## 3. Offer Help
Helping others often takes more effort than thanking or complimenting, but it builds the strongest bonds.

Helping doesn’t have to mean:
- Taking on large tasks
- Staying late
- Sacrificing your own work

Small actions count:
- Helping carry something
- Pitching in briefly when someone is overloaded
- Supporting someone while chatting informally

Ignoring someone who’s struggling — even unintentionally — can feel isolating.

---

## Don’t Miss the Struggle Around You
Sometimes people are clearly under pressure, but no one steps in because:
- “I’m busy too”
- “Someone else will help”
- “It’s not my responsibility”

That mindset creates separation — not teamwork.

Teams succeed when people notice difficulty and respond, even in small ways.

---

## Make It Practical and Intentional
You can approach kindness in two simple ways:

### By Person
For each colleague, ask:
- Have I thanked them recently?
- Have I complimented something they do well?
- Have I offered to help them?

### By Activity
Each day, challenge yourself:
- Thank one person
- Compliment one person
- Offer help to one person

Even one action a day changes the team atmosphere over time.

---

## Is This Fake or Manipulative?
Some people worry that deliberate kindness feels artificial.

In reality:
- Behaviours form habits
- Habits become character

If you consciously choose to act supportively, and it becomes natural over time, you’re not being manipulative — you’re becoming a kinder, more effective teammate.

---

## Track and Reflect
A simple exercise:
- List key people you work with
- Note when you last thanked, complimented, or helped them
- Look for obvious gaps
- Make a plan to address them

Awareness alone often leads to change.

---

## Key Takeaway
Great team players don’t wait for someone else to create a positive culture.

They:
- Notice effort
- Acknowledge strengths
- Step in when someone struggles

Small, genuine acts of kindness improve morale, trust, and performance — and they start with you.

# Forgive Members of Your Team

## Why Forgiveness Matters in Teams
No team operates perfectly. Mistakes, delays, misunderstandings, and awkward interactions are inevitable — especially in teams that aim high, move fast, and take risks.

Being a strong team player includes one final, often overlooked responsibility: **learning when to forgive and move on**.

Without forgiveness, teams slow down — emotionally and practically.

---

## Mistakes Are Part of Progress
People will sometimes:
- Miss things
- Communicate poorly
- Make decisions without everyone present
- Reject ideas clumsily
- Underperform temporarily

These failures aren’t usually personal. They happen because:
- Personalities differ
- Time pressure increases
- Communication drops when things get busy (ironically when it’s most needed)

Expecting perfection discourages risk‑taking — and risk‑taking is essential for growth.

---

## Don’t Carry Emotional Baggage
Holding onto resentment, irritation, or unresolved frustration:
- Consumes mental energy
- Damages collaboration
- Reduces your own effectiveness

In reality, most of the damage is done to **you**, not the person you’re upset with.

Letting go is not weakness — it’s efficiency.

---

## When Letting Go Is the Right Choice
If an issue:
- Was likely a one‑off
- Had understandable causes
- Is unlikely to repeat
- Has already led to process improvement

Then the healthiest response is often to **forgive and release it**.

Teams benefit far more from positivity and momentum than from perfect fairness.

---

## When It *Is* Okay to Speak Up
Forgiveness does not mean silence in every situation.

If:
- You genuinely feel hurt
- A pattern may repeat
- The issue affects future work

Then it’s reasonable to:
- Share how you feel
- Clarify expectations
- Confirm that safeguards are now in place

The goal is resolution — not blame.

---

## Separate Team Issues from Personal History
Everyone carries emotional experiences from the past:
- Times they weren’t heard
- Decisions made without them
- Lack of closure in earlier conflicts

Sometimes current irritation is magnified by old, unrelated baggage.

Ask honestly:
> *Is this really a team problem — or is it touching something unresolved from my past?*

Awareness here prevents unnecessary conflict.

---

## Choose the Outcome That Helps the Team
A simple guiding question:
> *What response produces the best result for the team right now?*

Often the answer is:
- Maintaining goodwill
- Preserving trust
- Moving forward constructively

Perfection is less important than progress.

---

## Reflection Exercise
Take a moment to consider:
- What has annoyed or hurt you recently in a team situation?
- Was it intentional — or circumstantial?
- Would forgiving it free up energy and focus?
- Would moving on benefit both you and the team?

Forgiveness can feel surprisingly relieving when practiced consciously.

---

## Key Takeaway
Great team players don’t just contribute, communicate, and support others — they also **forgive intelligently**.

Letting go of minor flaws:
- Protects team morale
- Preserves momentum
- Reduces your own stress

Forgiveness isn’t about excusing poor behavior — it’s about choosing progress over resentment.
