# How to Monitor Project Progress

## Why Progress Monitoring Matters
The only reliable way to know whether a project is on schedule is to **compare real progress against the plan**.  
This is the purpose of step nine in the project lifecycle.

Without monitoring, problems remain hidden until the end—when it is expensive, stressful, or impossible to fix them.

---

## The Core Method: Update the Gantt Chart
Progress is monitored by **coloring in the Gantt chart** to show completed work.

This involves:
- Filling in the parts of task bars that are finished
- Comparing progress to the constantly advancing **“today” line**

If progress keeps pace with the today line, the project is on track.  
If it falls behind, action may be needed.

---

## Why This Is Often Skipped
Many teams:
- Create a detailed Gantt chart to gain approval
- Archive it once the project starts
- Rely on informal updates instead

This wastes one of the simplest and most powerful project management tools.

---

## The Danger of Not Monitoring
If progress is not checked regularly:
- Delays become invisible
- Problems accumulate quietly
- Reality only becomes clear when the deadline is close

At that point, recovery requires drastic measures or unpleasant surprises for stakeholders.

---

## Why Visual Tracking Works
A colored‑in Gantt chart instantly answers the key question:
> “Are we where we should be by now?”

Status stories explain *what happened*, but not whether the project is **ahead, on time, or behind**.

---

## Review Rhythm
- Long projects → Monthly updates
- Short projects → Weekly updates

For long tasks:
- Use **sub‑Gantt charts** to track partial progress
- Color in long bars proportionally

When delays appear—especially on the critical path—you still have time to respond.

---

## The One‑Minute Test
If there were time for only one project question, it would be:
> “Can I see your colored‑in Gantt chart?”

If that chart is current, everything else can be discussed efficiently.

---

# The Six Combinations of Progress and Spend

## Why Cost Alone Is Misleading
Step ten focuses on cost control, but spending numbers are meaningless without progress context.

An apparent overspend may be acceptable if extra work has been done.  
An apparent underspend may hide serious delay.

---

## The Six Performance Patterns
Below are six common scenarios, ordered from worst to best from a financial controller’s perspective.

---

### 1. Heavily Overspent, Slightly Ahead
- Spending looks alarming
- Extra progress explains part of the overshoot

The true overrun is smaller than the financial report suggests.

---

### 2. Slightly Overspent, Far Ahead
- Costs slightly higher than planned
- Significantly more work completed

May actually be good performance—unless expensive work remains.

---

### 3. Overspent and Behind Schedule ❌
- One of the most dangerous states
- Some overspend is hidden by lack of progress

Recovering schedule will usually worsen costs.

---

### 4. On Budget but Behind
- Financially “perfect”
- Actually late and overspent in value terms

Common in people‑only projects where time is being consumed without progress.

---

### 5. Early Heavy Spend and Very Late 🚨
- Appears acceptable financially
- Actually a major problem

Often caused by expensive early tasks overrunning badly.  
Impossible to detect without a Gantt chart.

---

### 6. Slightly Behind but Underspent ✅
- Time pressure exists
- Budget headroom allows recovery options

This situation offers flexibility.

---

## Key Insight
Always judge spending **in relation to progress**, not in isolation.

The Gantt chart provides the missing half of the story.

---

# How to Forecast Future Cost and Completion

## From Status to Forecast
Knowing the current position is useful, but stakeholders really want to know:
- Where will we finish?
- How much will it ultimately cost?
- When will we actually complete?

Forecasting requires judgment, not just arithmetic.

---

## The Critical Question
Are current issues:
- **One‑off problems** that are now resolved, or
- **Systemic issues** likely to continue?

Only the project manager can make this call.

---

## Simple Forecast Example
- Planned total cost: 100
- Halfway point:
  - Planned spend: 50
  - Actual spend: 55

Possible outcomes:
- If problems repeat → final cost ≈ 110
- If problems are resolved → final cost ≈ 105

---

## Practical Forecasting Rules
- If overspending early → assume it continues
- If underspending early → expect return to plan
- If unsure → assume the worst‑case trend

Optimism without evidence damages credibility.

---

## Valid Exceptions
You can assume recovery only if:
- The cause was clearly non‑repeatable
- The issue has been fully addressed
- Evidence supports a change in trend

Examples:
- One‑off startup delay
- Single approved scope change

---

## Same Logic for Schedule
Time forecasts follow the same rules as cost:
- Early delay often repeats
- Conservative estimates preserve trust

---

## Summary
Strong project control depends on:
- Regularly updated Gantt charts
- Progress‑based cost interpretation
- Honest forecasting
- Early visibility of problems

The colored‑in Gantt chart is the single most powerful tool for achieving all of this.

# When to Re‑Adjust the Project Plan

## Why Re‑Planning Is Normal
Almost no project finishes:
- Exactly on time
- Exactly on budget
- With no changes at all

Progress monitoring tells you *what* is happening.  
Re‑adjusting the plan is about deciding **what to promise from here onward**.

---

## The Key Decision Point
The difficult question is not:
> “Can we still somehow recover?”

But:
> “Is it still realistic to keep promising the original date and cost?”

Hope tends to shrink as projects progress—and hope alone is not a plan.

---

## The Best Timing: The Middle Third

A useful rule is to divide the project into thirds.

### First Third (0–33%)
Too early to re‑plan.

- Asking for more time or money at this stage looks like poor planning
- Early delays should be absorbed and recovered internally
- The aim is to catch up in later tasks without disturbing the end date

✅ **Exception**  
If the **customer adds scope**, request extra time and money immediately and confirm it in writing.

---

### Middle Third (34–66%) ✅ Ideal Window
This is the **right time** to re‑adjust if needed.

Why:
- Enough work has been done to see real trends
- There is still time to change direction
- Stakeholders can realistically rebook time and resources

For a nine‑month project, this means months four, five, and six.

---

### Final Third (67–100%)
Usually too late.

- Customers dislike last‑minute surprises
- Options for recovery are limited
- Even justified delays often fall on the project manager

Waiting for a miracle late in the project usually makes the outcome worse.

---

## Practical Guideline
Have an open, honest conversation:
- Around **halfway through**
- Definitely **before 70% completion**

Early honesty is uncomfortable—but far less damaging than late disclosure.

---

# What to Do When a Project Is Late or Over Budget

## If the Project Is Running Late (Schedule Delay)

There are **six possible actions**:

### 1. Increase Resources (Money Up)
- Add people
- Use overtime
- Hire contractors or special equipment
- Pay for faster logistics

This can recover time but always increases cost.

---

### 2. Reduce Scope or Quality
- Remove non‑essential features
- Simplify deliverables

This must ideally be agreed with the customer.  
Silent quality reduction is risky.

---

### 3. Overlap Remaining Tasks
- Run tasks in parallel that were previously sequential

This saves time without direct cost increase but adds risk and complexity.

---

### 4. Allow the Project to Slip
- If time is not the key driver
- Ideally agreed with the customer

Often easier than expected, especially if delays are partly outside your control.

---

### 5. Do Nothing and Hope ❌
Included only because it happens so often.

Projects almost always continue behaving as they have so far.

---

### 6. Stop the Project
- Cancel or abandon the work

Not desirable, but sometimes better than continuing an unviable effort.

---

## If the Project Is Over Budget

There are fewer options:

### Option A: Reduce Scope or Quality
Trim remaining work to fit the available budget.

### Option B: Accept the Overspend
- Ask for more funding, or
- Accept reduced profit or a loss

Neither is attractive—but they are realities.

---

## The Real Lesson
The pain of this step is reduced by:
- Thorough task identification (Step 2)
- Realistic estimates
- Adequate contingency for time and cost

Good planning doesn’t eliminate hard choices—but it reduces their severity.

# Why People Avoid Reviewing Projects

## The Importance of Reviews
Project reviews (Step 12) exist to:
- Capture learning
- Improve future performance
- Celebrate success
- Thank contributors

Yet they are often skipped.

---

## Seven Common Reasons Reviews Are Avoided

### 1. “There’s No Time”
Short‑term urgency wins—but long‑term efficiency suffers.

---

### 2. “I Don’t Want to Relive the Pain”
Avoiding reflection ensures the same pain returns later.

---

### 3. “I Don’t Want to Admit Mistakes”
Fear of blame blocks learning.

Solution:
- Keep reviews open and supportive
- Focus on systems, not individuals
- Avoid naming people in written notes

---

### 4. “It’s All in My Head”
Memory fades and cannot be shared.

Written learning benefits the entire organization.

---

### 5. “It Went Well, So We Don’t Need One”
Success is actually the **best** time to review:
- What worked can be repeated
- Lucky outcomes can be understood

---

### 6. “We’ll Never Do This Again”
Similar projects always return—often under different names.

---

### 7. “Nobody Will Read It”
Reviews only become valuable once a collection exists.

The habit must come before the payoff.

---

# How to Review a Project

## Set the Right Tone
Mark project completion positively:
- Order food
- Meet informally
- Signal appreciation and closure

This encourages openness rather than defensiveness.

---

## The Three Review Questions

### 1. What Went Well?
Identify actions and decisions worth repeating.

---

### 2. What Went Badly?
Discuss problems without blame.
Thank people for discovering issues early.

---

### 3. What Could We Do Differently Next Time?
This is about **missed opportunities**, not mistakes.

Perfect hindsight lasts briefly—use it.

---

## Support the Discussion
- Use meeting notes, issue logs, or reports to jog memory
- Capture insights live

The output should be:
- Short
- Clear
- Easy to re‑read

One page is usually enough.

---

## Store the Learning
Save reviews in a shared, searchable location.

Some organizations:
- Require a review before closing a project

This is one form worth keeping.

---

# Applying the Simple 12‑Step Process

## What the Process Proves
Project management is:
- Practical
- Logical
- Repeatable

At its core:
- List tasks
- Sequence them
- Build a Gantt chart
- Monitor progress
- Control costs
- Review and learn

---

## How to Make It Stick
The best way to remember the process is to **use it immediately**:
- Pick a messy or delayed project
- Plan what remains
- Color in what’s already done

Even a half‑project plan adds clarity.

---

## Final Thought
This process works:
- For small projects
- For large projects
- For professional and personal work

Once learned, it’s useful for life.

Good luck with your future projects.
