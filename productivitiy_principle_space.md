# Taking an Inventory of Your Gathering Points

## Introducing the Principle of Space
Improving productivity begins with controlling **space** — both physical and digital. The first step is becoming aware of where unfinished work accumulates.

To do that, you need to identify your **gathering points**.

---

## What Is a Gathering Point?
A gathering point is any place where **unprocessed items** collect.

An item is unprocessed if even one of the following is missing:
- You haven’t decided **what** to do with it
- You haven’t decided **when** to do it
- You haven’t decided **where** it belongs

If any of those are unclear, the item is unresolved — and it creates mental friction.

---

## Why Gathering Points Matter
Gathering points are everywhere:
- Paper piles
- Email inboxes
- Notes apps
- Drawers
- Desktops
- Messaging apps

The more gathering points you have:
- The more attention switches you make
- The more time you lose
- The more stress you experience

Every gathering point adds hidden switching cost.

---

## Completing the Gathering Points Inventory
Use the provided worksheet to count your gathering points.

For each category:
- Count how many separate locations you use
- Record the total

Examples:
- Three email inboxes → enter “3”
- Two paper piles on your desk → enter “2”

Pause and complete the worksheet before going further.

---

## Typical Results (What to Expect)
Most people are surprised by the number they discover.

Before using this system:
- Average: **30–40 gathering points**
- Exceptionally organized individuals: **single digits**
- Highly chaotic cases: **over 100**

Your number is not a judgment — it’s an opportunity.

A higher count means greater potential time savings.

---

## The Target: Six or Fewer
The goal is not perfection — it’s reduction.

Everyone is working toward:
> **Six or fewer approved gathering points**

You’re already moving in the right direction.

---

# Narrowing Your Gathering Points

## Shift from Many to Few
To reduce switchtasking and improve focus, intentionally choose a small number of **approved gathering points**.

Anything outside these becomes unapproved.

Fewer gathering points = fewer attention switches.

---

## The Six Approved Gathering Points

### 1. Physical Inbox
A single location for all unprocessed physical items:
- Papers
- Receipts
- Objects
- Tools

Everything unresolved goes here — nothing gets sorted immediately.

---

### 2. Portable Inbox
A mobile extension of your physical inbox.

This could be:
- One pocket in a bag
- A folder
- A phone case slot

Anything handed to you while out goes here — until you return.

---

### 3. Notepad (Paper or Digital)
Your capture tool for:
- Ideas
- Notes
- Action items

Choose **one** notepad only.

Mark action items clearly so they can be processed later.

---

### 4. Email Inbox
Ideally, one combined email inbox.

Even if you have multiple accounts, reduce the number of apps you check.

Fewer inboxes = less switching.

---

### 5. Messaging App
Choose one primary messaging platform for work communication.

If necessary, separate true emergencies into another channel — but be intentional.

---

### 6. Wild Card (Optional)
A flexible slot for special needs, such as:
- Voicemail (if it doesn’t forward)
- A tasks app
- A specific social platform
- A personal assistant acting as a gatekeeper

Use only if necessary.

---

# Setting Up Each Gathering Point

## Setting Up a Physical Inbox
Choose **one large container** for all physical, unprocessed items.

Key rules:
- Anything unresolved goes in
- Do not put items away immediately
- Empty it at scheduled processing times

Avoid “whirlwind organization” — occasional stressful clean‑ups.

Bigger is better. Overflow causes avoidance.

---

## Working with a Portable Inbox
Carry one defined pocket or folder for items received on the go.

When you return:
- Empty it into your physical inbox

If you go mostly paperless:
- Take photos
- Save items to your digital notepad
- Or use the portable inbox as a temporary staging area

---

## Getting the Most from a Notepad
Choose **paper or digital**, not both.

Best practices:
- Only one active notepad
- Mark action items clearly (e.g., open checkboxes)
- For paper notepads: tear out pages and place them in your physical inbox
- Archive notes after processing

Your notepad gathers — it does not manage.

---

## Reducing Multiple Email Accounts
The objective is flow, not elimination.

If possible:
- Combine multiple accounts into one inbox

If separation is mandated:
- Reduce to the fewest inboxes allowed

Even reducing from five to two brings significant benefits.

---

## Choosing Your Messaging App
Messaging is a major source of switchtasking.

Tips:
- Reduce the number of messaging apps
- Set expectations for response time
- Consider reserving text messages for emergencies only
- Route messages to another gathering point if possible

If a conversation becomes complex, switch to a call.

---

## Picking a Wild Card Gathering Point
The wild card exists for flexibility.

Possible examples:
- Voicemail
- A task list
- Social platform messages
- A scheduling assistant

Avoid using people (friends/family) as reminders — they are not gathering points.

Keep the wild card limited.

---

## Separating Work and Personal Gathering Points
Combining work and personal gathering points:
- Reduces switching
- Saves time
- Improves focus

In many cases, you can:
- Process personal tasks at work
- Schedule completion outside work hours

If separation is required:
- That’s okay
- A few extra gathering points won’t ruin the system

Progress matters more than perfection.
