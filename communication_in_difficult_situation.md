# Communication for Career Success
## When You Are Nervous

---

## Nervousness Is Normal
A racing heart, tense stomach, or shaky voice are common before speaking.  
Some people feel anxious in front of large audiences, while others struggle more with executives, authority figures, or sensitive topics.

The goal is not to eliminate nerves, but to **manage them effectively**.

---

## The BREATHE Framework
Use the acronym **BREATHE** to remember practical techniques that reduce anxiety and strengthen your vocal presence.

---

## B — Breathe and Prepare Physically
Just before you speak:
- Find a private spot
- Stand tall and take up space
- Stretch your body
- Take slow, deep breaths

Expanding your posture and breathing deeply helps your body shift into a more confident state.

---

## R — Reframe Your Inner Dialogue
Nervousness and excitement create similar physical sensations.

Instead of thinking:
- “I’m terrified”
- “My nerves are out of control”

Try reframing:
- “I’m energized”
- “I’m excited about this opportunity”

Your body often believes the story your mind tells it.

---

## E — Ease Into Your Fear
Gradual exposure makes scary situations less intimidating.

Ways to practice:
- Move closer to the front of a class or group setting
- Speak up briefly in meetings while seated
- Slowly increase visibility as comfort grows

Small steps build tolerance and confidence over time.

---

## A — Alternate Your Practice
Avoid rehearsing the same way every time.

Try:
- Practicing with visuals
- Explaining your topic to a friend
- Talking through it mentally while commuting
- Recording yourself and listening back

Practice different sections out of order so interruptions won’t derail you.

---

## T — Terminate Negative Self‑Talk
Watch for unhelpful thoughts like:
- “I’m going to mess this up”
- “Everyone will judge me”

When they appear:
- Pause
- Replace them with productive statements

Examples:
- “This is how I share my expertise”
- “I’m prepared and knowledgeable”

Thoughts shape confidence.

---

## H — Heart Rate Up Practice
Simulate stress during practice.

How:
- Walk briskly or climb stairs
- Practice speaking while your heart rate is elevated

This trains you to manage breath and voice even when adrenaline rises.

---

## E — Empathize With Your Audience
Fear often grows when focus stays on yourself.

Shift attention outward:
- What does the audience need?
- How can your message help them?
- What would make this easier for them to follow?

Empathy redirects energy away from self‑judgment and toward connection.

---

## Putting It All Together
When nerves appear:
- Pause
- Breathe
- Work through **BREATHE** mentally

These tools help you stay steady, present, and effective.

---

## Key Takeaway
Nerves don’t mean you’re unprepared — they mean you care.

> When you manage your body, thoughts, and focus, nervous energy becomes communication power.

# Communication for Career Success
## Tool Kit: Managing Nervousness

---

## Purpose of This Toolkit
Managing nervousness is most effective when you **prioritize techniques** and **apply them to a real situation**.  
This toolkit helps you move from knowing the strategies to actually using them.

---

## Step 1: Identify a Triggering Event
Open your calendar and locate an upcoming event that makes you feel uneasy.

Examples:
- A presentation
- A difficult conversation
- A meeting with senior leaders
- A virtual session involving technology

Write the event down so your plan is specific.

---

## Step 2: Choose Your Two Most Helpful Strategies
From the seven BREATHE strategies, select **two** that best fit your situation:

- Breathing and physical preparation  
- Reframing internal dialogue  
- Easing into fear  
- Alternating practice methods  
- Terminating negative self‑talk  
- Practicing with an elevated heart rate  
- Empathizing with the audience  

Focusing on just two keeps the plan realistic and manageable.

---

## Step 3: Create a Personalized Action Plan
For each chosen strategy, define **exact actions**.

### Example: Negative Self‑Talk
- Identify the unhelpful thought
- Replace it with a confident, realistic statement
- Write your new phrase down
- Place it where you’ll see it frequently (calendar, notes, reminders)

---

### Example: Practice Strategy
- Decide how to rehearse differently
- Add real‑world elements (technology, audience, interruptions)
- Schedule practice sessions in advance

Concrete actions turn anxiety into preparation.

---

## Step 4: Build Visibility Into Your Plan
Reinforcement matters.

Ways to reinforce:
- Add reminders to your calendar
- Use sticky notes or digital prompts
- Repeat your new message aloud during preparation

Consistency helps your brain accept the new narrative.

---

## Step 5: Commit and Follow Through
Before the event:
- Review your two strategies
- Run through the action steps
- Trust the preparation you’ve done

Nervousness may still appear — that’s normal.  
You now have tools to manage it.

---

## Reflection Prompt
After the event, reflect:
- Which strategy helped the most?
- What would you repeat next time?
- What would you adjust?

Confidence grows through reflection, not perfection.

---

## Key Takeaway
Nervousness decreases when preparation becomes intentional and personal.

> Choose your tools, plan your actions, and confidence will follow.

# Communication for Career Success
## When You Disagree With Someone

---

## Disagreement Is Inevitable
Disagreements at work are unavoidable. They may arise around:
- Work processes or decisions
- Values, priorities, or beliefs
- Social or political topics

The key challenge is learning how to **express disagreement respectfully** while staying true to your values.

---

## Two Questions to Ask Before Speaking Up

### 1. How Valuable Is the Relationship?
Before engaging, consider:
- Is this relationship fragile or strong?
- Can it withstand opposing viewpoints?
- Would this disagreement cause lasting harm?

Not every disagreement needs to be voiced. Choose carefully.

---

### 2. What Are the Power Dynamics?
Power affects how disagreement is received.

Examples:
- Leaders who dismiss differing views may appear controlling
- Disagreeing with a manager requires extra tact
- Silence may sometimes be strategic, not weak

Awareness helps you select the most effective response.

---

## Choose Your Battles Wisely
There is no universal rule for handling every disagreement.  
Effectiveness depends on context, relationship strength, and influence dynamics.

---

## A Three‑Step Framework for Disagreement

### Step 1: Prepare
Before a difficult conversation:
- Identify assumptions you may be making
- List three possible positive intentions behind the other person’s view

Most people are acting from logic or values, not malice.

A useful strategy:
- Ignore your first reaction (often defensive)
- Ignore your second reaction (often accusatory)
- Lead with your third reaction — it is usually more thoughtful and solution‑focused

Practice your response with a trusted colleague if possible.

---

### Step 2: Pace the Conversation
How you engage matters as much as what you say.

During the conversation:
- Be mindful of your emotional state
- Control your speaking pace and tone
- Ask genuine questions to understand the other perspective
- Acknowledge their position without agreeing
- Share your viewpoint calmly and factually
- Avoid judgmental or absolute language

Listen closely and stay flexible. Appreciation goes a long way.

---

### Step 3: Process Afterward
What happens after matters too.

After the conversation:
- Reflect on how it went
- Consider what worked and what didn’t
- Shift focus toward:
  - Problem solving
  - Mutual understanding
  - Possible next steps

Growth happens through reflection and adjustment.

---

## The Risk of Staying Silent
Avoiding disagreement can feel safer, but silence carries its own cost.

When concerns go unspoken:
- Problems persist
- Trust erodes internally
- Opportunities for improvement are lost

Respectful engagement is often healthier than avoidance.

---

## Reflection Questions
Before your next disagreement, ask:
- Is this worth raising?
- What assumptions am I making?
- What outcome do I want from this conversation?
- How can I express my view without escalating tension?

---

## Key Takeaway
Disagreement handled thoughtfully can strengthen relationships and decisions.

> Prepare intentionally, pace yourself in the moment, and process afterward to grow as a communicator.
