# 2. Identify and Manage Risks to Avoid Project Failure

### ‚ö†Ô∏è Why Risk Management Matters
Projects carry many uncertainties.  
**Proactive** risk management:
- Improves success rates  
- Helps control outcomes  
- Reduces negative impact  

### üìò PMI Definition of Risk
> ‚ÄúAn uncertain event or condition that, if it occurs, has a positive or negative effect on one or more project objectives.‚Äù

### üß© Types of Risks
1. **Known Unknowns**  
   - You know they exist, but not the impact.  
   - Example: Fuel prices will increase, but by how much?

2. **Unknown Unknowns**  
   - Events you cannot anticipate.  
   - Examples:
     - Natural disasters  
     - Sudden loss of key personnel

# 3. The Risk Management Process

### 1Ô∏è‚É£ Create a Risk Management Plan
Include:
- **Risk appetite** (risk tolerance of stakeholders)  
- **Risk strategy** (reactive vs proactive approach)  
- **Roles & responsibilities** (risk owner, risk lead)  
- **Risk categories** (internal, external, equipment, human resources, etc.)  
- **Definitions for probability & impact**
  - High / Medium / Low  
- **Probability and impact matrix**  
  - Used to evaluate each risk consistently

---

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ Identify Risks
Use:
- Brainstorming  
- Interviews  
- Checklists  
- Risk categories as prompts  

Goal: get **as many risks on the table as possible**.

---

### 3Ô∏è‚É£ Analyze Risks

#### Step A: Assess Probability & Impact
For each risk ask:
- What is the probability of this occurring?  
- What is the impact if it happens?  

Assign numerical values, e.g.:
- High = 5  
- Medium = 3  
- Low = 1  

Multiply to create a **priority score**.

**Example:**  
High probability (5) √ó High impact (5) = **25** ‚Üí top priority

#### Step B: Quantify Risks
Used to calculate:
- Cost impacts  
- Schedule impacts  
- Overall exposure  

Example:
- Risk: 4‚Äëweek delay due to customs  
- Cost impact: $50,000  

Focus on the **high-probability / high-impact** risks if the list is large.

## Estimating Techniques

### 1Ô∏è‚É£ Analogous Estimating (Top‚ÄëDown)
- Based on past similar projects  
- Fast and simple  
- Least accurate  
**Example:** ‚ÄúLast year‚Äôs project cost $200,000‚Äîthis one will be similar.‚Äù

---

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ Parametric Estimating
Uses formulas based on measurable parameters.  
Works best when activities are **linear**.

**Example:**
- 1 mile paved per hour at $1,000 per mile  
- 10 miles ‚Üí 10 hours, $10,000

---

### 3Ô∏è‚É£ Bottom‚ÄëUp Estimating
- Estimate every activity from the lowest level  
- Add them up to form totals  
- Time-consuming but **most accurate**

---

### 4Ô∏è‚É£ Three-Point Estimating
Uses three scenarios:
- **Optimistic (O)**
- **Most likely (M)**
- **Pessimistic (P)**

Two calculation methods:

#### Beta Distribution (PERT)  
Weighted formula:
\[
(O + 4M + P) / 6
\]
More accurate.

**Example:**
- O = 3 days  
- M = 7 days  
- P = 15 days  
- Result = **7.67 days**

---

#### Triangular Distribution  
Simpler but less accurate:
\[
(O + M + P) / 3
\]

Example result: **8.33 days**
