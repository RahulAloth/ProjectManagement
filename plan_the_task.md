# Three Ways to List All Project Tasks

## Why Task Listing Matters
The second step in effective project management is breaking the project into **clear, manageable tasks**.  
Your task list forms the foundation of the entire plan.

If tasks are missing:
- The schedule will be longer than expected
- Costs will be higher than planned
- The final output may fall short of expectations

Because projects usually involve unfamiliar work, identifying *all* tasks requires a structured approach.

---

## Method 1: Creative Brainstorming

Start with imagination rather than structure.

### How It Works
- Bring the team together
- Encourage free‑flowing ideas
- Capture *everything* without criticism or filtering
- Organize later

### Why This Helps
- Increases early engagement and ownership
- Draws on different perspectives and concerns
- Surfaces ideas that a logical method might miss

At this stage, completeness matters more than order.

---

## Method 2: Logical Structuring (Work Breakdown Structure)

Once the raw ideas are captured, switch to analysis.

### What This Means
Group tasks into logical categories and progressively break them down into smaller parts.

Example (construction):
- Structure (foundations, walls, roof)
- Internal spaces (rooms, kitchens, bathrooms)
- Services (electrical, plumbing, heating)
- External work (landscaping, access)

This hierarchical task map is commonly known as a **Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)**.

### Benefits
- Makes the project easier to understand
- Reveals missing tasks
- Provides a clean, shareable structure

Despite the formal name, it is simply an organized view of the work.

---

## Method 3: Learn from Others and from History

Avoid reinventing the wheel.

### Sources to Use
- Internal experts
- External consultants
- Teams who completed similar projects
- Past project documents or schedules

Even a simple Gantt chart from a previous project can reveal tasks you might otherwise overlook.

---

## Using the Methods Together
You don’t have to apply all three in every case, but accuracy improves when you combine them.

Examples:
- Familiar projects → start from past plans
- Large projects → brainstorm each major WBS branch
- Complex projects → use all three methods

The goal is **coverage**, not elegance.

---

# Choosing the Right Level of Granularity

## What Is Granularity?
Granularity refers to **how detailed your tasks are**.

- Too much detail → the plan becomes unmanageable
- Too little detail → estimates become unreliable

There is no exact formula, but there are strong indicators of when tasks are the wrong size.

---

## Sign 1: You Can’t Estimate Time or Cost
If you cannot reasonably estimate how long a task will take or how much it will cost, it’s too large.

Example:
- “Install kitchen” is too vague
- It must be broken down into clearer components

However, tasks shouldn’t be broken down to trivial actions either. The aim is *useful* detail.

---

## Sign 2: Tasks Overlap in Reality
If one task could begin before another fully finishes, they are probably too big.

Example:
- Digging a long trench
- Installing pipes

In reality, pipe installation can begin before the entire trench is complete.  
The solution is to split large tasks into stages that reflect real sequencing.

---

## Sign 3: The Forbidden “O‑Word” (Ongoing)
Tasks described as **“ongoing”** are a warning sign.

### Why This Is a Problem
- No clear measurement of progress
- Encourages delay at the start
- Causes panic near the deadline

Example:
- Electrical work marked as “ongoing” during a build

### Better Approach
Break ongoing activities into **time‑boxed, measurable tasks** (e.g. weekly work packages).  
This allows progress to be tracked and controlled.

---

## Finding the Sweet Spot
Good task granularity means:
- Tasks are small enough to estimate
- Tasks reflect how work actually happens
- Tasks are easy to track and review

The rule of thumb:
> Keep breaking tasks down until you can confidently estimate and control them — but no further.

---

## Summary
To build a reliable task list:
- Use creative thinking
- Apply logical structure
- Learn from experience
- Choose a practical level of detail

Well‑defined tasks give you accuracy, control, and confidence throughout the project.

# Using Post‑its to Plan the Order of Tasks

## Why Task Sequencing Matters
This step is the **core of project planning**. It’s where individual tasks are turned into a coherent workflow.

At this point, a project typically moves from:
- “I don’t really know how this will work”  
to  
- “I understand the order, the starting point, and the likely duration”

This clarity is powerful and reassuring.

---

## Building the Task Order with Post‑its

### The Basic Idea
Each task is written on a separate Post‑it note. These notes are then arranged to show:
- What must happen first
- What can run in parallel
- What depends on something else being finished

Arrows are drawn between notes to show dependencies:
- An arrow means *this task cannot start until the previous one is complete*

Only the first and last tasks will have no incoming or outgoing arrows.

---

## Why Post‑its Work So Well
Although it looks low‑tech, this method has major advantages:

- Tasks can be moved quickly and easily
- The whole plan is visible at once
- Dependencies are immediately obvious
- Collaboration is natural and effective

This approach works best with **two or three colleagues**:
- It’s engaging and intuitive
- People contribute ideas even without project management knowledge
- Team members feel ownership of the plan
- Motivation improves later during execution

Unlike software tools, Post‑its encourage discussion rather than control.

---

## There Is More Than One “Right” Plan
Project management is rarely about finding a single perfect answer.

Instead, it’s about:
- Creating an approach the team believes in
- Agreeing it with the customer
- Committing to it consistently

Different task orders can still lead to success, as long as the constraints are understood and accepted.

---

## Estimating Task Durations

### Adding Time to Each Task
Once the order is clear, assign a **reasonable average duration** to every task:
- Often expressed in weeks
- Large, clearly written numbers work best
- Using a different color helps visibility

### When You Don’t Know the Duration
- Ask someone with experience
- Consult a specialist or expert
- If no one can estimate it, break the task down further until you reach parts you understand

If a task can’t be estimated, it isn’t defined clearly enough.

---

## Understanding the Critical Path

## What the Critical Path Is
The critical path is:
> **The longest sequence of dependent tasks in the project**

This path determines the **minimum possible duration** of the entire project.

Tasks on this path are called **critical tasks**.

---

## What “Critical” Really Means
The word *critical* is misleading.

- It does **not** mean “important” or “difficult”
- It simply means **time‑critical**

A task can be:
- Simple and inexpensive, yet critical  
- Highly important, yet non‑critical

Only the task relationships reveal this — you cannot tell by intuition alone.

---

## Why the Critical Path Is So Important
Critical tasks:
- Define the promised completion date
- Have **zero tolerance for delay**
- Must be estimated accurately
- Need close monitoring

If any critical task runs late, the entire project finishes late.

Non‑critical tasks have **float** (spare time):
- They can slip slightly without affecting the final date

---

## Managing Critical vs Non‑Critical Tasks

### For Critical Tasks
- Assign your best people
- Track progress closely
- Use realistic time estimates

### For Non‑Critical Tasks
- Monitor lightly
- Be aware that changes can shift the critical path

If you shorten or speed up a non‑critical task, a different path may become the longest — creating a **new critical path**.

---

## Why Post‑its Are Ideal for This Step
Post‑its allow you to:
- Visualize the task order
- Add realistic durations
- Identify the longest path instantly
- Adjust the plan easily when circumstances change

Finding the critical path becomes straightforward and visual rather than abstract.

---

## Summary
Using Post‑its to plan task order helps you:
- Build a shared understanding of the project
- Create a flexible, logical schedule
- Discover the critical path
- Focus attention where delays really matter

It is quick, intuitive, collaborative — and extremely effective.
