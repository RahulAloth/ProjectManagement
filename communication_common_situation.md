# Communication for Career Success
## When You Give an Update

---

## What Is an Update?
An update is essentially a **short, focused presentation**.  
It often happens unexpectedly in meetings, but it still requires structure, clarity, and relevance.

The goal of an update is not to report everything — it is to deliver **value** quickly.

---

## Think of an Update as a Mini Presentation
Even when you are put on the spot, organize your response mentally.  
Before speaking, anchor your update around **three key questions**.

---

## Question 1: What’s the One Thing You Want Them to Remember?
This is your **headline**.

- It should be memorable
- It should capture attention
- It should set the direction for the rest of your update

Focus on the *most important takeaway*, not a full summary.  
If you remember only one idea, your listeners likely will too.

---

## Question 2: Why Should Your Audience Care?
Shift from *what you experienced* to *what it means for them*.

Consider:
- What do stakeholders value?
- How does this connect to business goals?
- How does it improve performance, efficiency, or outcomes?

This step helps you filter out interesting but irrelevant details.

---

## Question 3: What Are the Supporting Points?
Information is easier to remember when it follows a pattern.

A useful guideline:
- Group your ideas into **about three key points**
- Support each point with a brief explanation or example

This makes your message structured, concise, and easier to follow.

---

## Making Your Update Memorable
To strengthen retention:
- Use clear categories
- Add a quick “for example” or “for instance”
- Tie examples directly back to your main point

Relevance matters more than volume.

---

## How to Close an Update
Since an update is a mini presentation, it should have a clear ending.

An effective wrap‑up:
- Reinforces your opening headline
- Summarizes the overall value
- Invites follow‑up questions or discussion

This signals confidence and opens the door for engagement.

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Listing everything you did or saw
- Focusing on logistics instead of impact
- Speaking without a clear takeaway
- Ending abruptly without a summary

---

## Reflection Prompt
Before your next update, ask yourself:
- What is my headline?
- Why does this matter to my audience?
- What three points will help them remember this?

---

## Key Takeaway
An effective update is short, intentional, and audience‑focused.

> Treat every update as a mini presentation, and you’ll sound prepared — even when you’re not.

# Communication for Career Success
## Tool Kit: Giving an Update

---

## Purpose of This Toolkit
This toolkit helps you practice giving clear, confident updates so you’re ready when asked for:
- A progress report
- A summary of work
- A quick recap in a meeting

The goal is preparation through repetition.

---

## Drill 1: Practice Creating Headlines
Train yourself to respond with a **short, memorable headline**.

For each prompt, think of **2–3 words** only:
- How was your weekend?
- What’s the status of your current project?
- Where would you travel if money were not a factor?
- What came out of the last department meeting?

Notice which topics feel easier. This helps you recognize where you need more practice.

---

## Drill 2: Customize for Your Audience
Strong updates are always audience‑focused.

### Step 1
Choose **3–4 colleagues** you work with regularly.

### Step 2
For each person, write down:
- What they care about
- What they value (speed, innovation, cost, risk, learning, etc.)

### Step 3
Take one experience (e.g., a conference, project, or meeting) and:
- Identify the highlight that would matter most to each person
- Rewrite the same update differently for each audience

This builds flexibility and awareness.

---

## Drill 3: Build Supporting Points
Once you have a headline, add structure.

For each prompt, list **three supporting points**:
- Weekend update → three favorite activities
- Project update → three progress buckets
- Travel choice → three destinations
- Meeting recap → three key takeaways

This trains your brain to organize ideas quickly.

---

## Using AI to Practice Updates
You can use technology to improve clarity and conciseness.

Ways to practice:
- Record yourself giving an update
- Review for unnecessary words
- Edit for sharper emphasis

If you don’t have a speaking‑specific tool:
- Dictate your update
- Paste it into a generative AI tool
- Ask it to remove filler and strengthen key points

Practice improves both **credibility** and **speaking presence**.

---

## Key Takeaway
Regular practice with headlines, audience focus, and structured points prepares you to give strong updates — even on the spot.

# Communication for Career Success
## When You Attend Meetings

---

## Why Meetings Matter
Professionals spend a significant part of their work life in meetings, yet many meetings fail to fully use the knowledge in the room.

Your goal:
- Increase visibility
- Add value
- Improve decision quality

---

## Increase Your Visibility in Meetings

### Before the Meeting
- Arrive early
- Choose a central seat (corners reduce perceived presence)
- If virtual, keep your camera on
- Greet people by name as they arrive

These small actions make you more noticeable and approachable.

---

### During the Meeting
- Maintain an open, attentive posture
- Adjust seat or camera height to show engagement
- Make brief small talk to build rapport
- Use people’s names when responding

Show attentiveness through note‑taking, but be aware that on camera this can look like distraction.

---

## Improve Meeting Effectiveness

### The Common Knowledge Problem
Groups often focus on information everyone already knows.
Unique insights held by individuals frequently go unheard.

This leads to weaker decisions.

---

### What You Can Do
- Acknowledge that shared information isn’t the whole picture
- Invite new perspectives explicitly
- Ask for information that hasn’t been mentioned yet

Example prompts:
- “What hasn’t surfaced yet?”
- “Does anyone have a different angle?”

---

### Read Non‑Verbal Cues
Notice when someone looks thoughtful or hesitant.
Create space by inviting them to speak.

This helps unlock valuable insights.

---

### Protect Unique Contributions
When someone shares new information:
- Acknowledge it
- Reinforce its importance
- Prevent it from being dismissed

This encourages future participation.

---

### Balance Participation
Use the **“three‑then‑me” rule**:
- After you contribute, wait until at least three others speak before speaking again

This supports inclusive dialogue and better decisions.

---

## Key Takeaway
Meetings improve when visibility increases **and** information diversity is encouraged.

> The best meetings surface what only a few people know.

## Self‑Assessment Exercise
Review your recent meetings and evaluate yourself honestly.

Ask:
- Do I arrive early and engage informally?
- Do I invite quieter voices into the conversation?
- Do I show visible attentiveness?
- Do I help surface unique information?

Identify what you already do well.

---

## Choose Two Improvement Actions
Select:
1. **One action** to increase your visibility and professional presence  
2. **One action** to improve inclusiveness and meeting effectiveness  

Keep these intentional and specific.

---

## Commit to Change
Open your calendar and find your **next five meetings**.

For each meeting:
- Add a note reminding yourself of your two chosen behaviors

This simple step increases follow‑through.

---

## Key Takeaway
Small, consistent behavior changes can dramatically improve:
- Your meeting presence
- Group decision making
- How others remember your contributions

> Better meetings begin with individual choices.
