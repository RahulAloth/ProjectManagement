# Understanding Work Time vs. You Time

## What Counts as “Work Time”?
Work time is broader than most people realize.

It includes:
- Hours spent actively working
- Commute or work‑related travel time
- Thinking about work outside office hours
- Side businesses, freelancing, or entrepreneurship

If your job pays for your thinking, then mental work counts — regardless of location.

---

## A Reality Check on Time Use
Many people underestimate how much they work.

For example:
- Being physically present with family
- While mentally focused on emails, deadlines, or projects

In this case, work is happening **in the presence of personal time**, not instead of it.

---

## Calculate Your Real Time Split
Try this simple exercise:

1. Estimate your **average weekly work time** (include thinking about work)
2. Subtract that number from **168 hours** (total hours in a week)
3. The remainder is **you time** (sleep, meals, relationships, rest, fun)

Awareness is the first step toward balance.

---

## Draw a Clear Boundary: The Finish Line
Lasting productivity doesn’t come from working endlessly — it comes from **working within boundaries**.

Create a scheduled end‑of‑day stopping point, called the **finish line**.

Why it works:
- Forces efficiency
- Encourages prioritization
- Sparks creativity within limits
- Prevents work from expanding endlessly

---

## Creating Your First Finish Line
Start gently:
- If you currently work until 7:00 pm, try 6:30 pm
- Or commit to no work on weekends

Add a recurring calendar entry labeled:
> “Finish Line – Work Ends”

This single boundary often improves both results and wellbeing.

# Establishing Your Most Valuable Activities (MVAs)

## Not All Work Is Equal
Jobs usually involve many tasks — but only a few create the majority of value.

To identify value:
- Think in *work terms*, not personal meaning
- Ask how much you’d pay someone else, per hour, to do this task at your standard

---

## Defining MVAs vs. LVAs
- **Most Valuable Activities (MVAs):**
  - High‑impact
  - Require your unique skills
  - Expensive to delegate

- **Least Valuable Activities (LVAs):**
  - Necessary but lower impact
  - Easier to delegate or delay

Most professionals spend **less than 20%** of their time on MVAs — despite MVAs driving most results.

---

## The Goal
To advance your career while working fewer hours:
> **Increase the percentage of time spent on MVAs.**

---

# Identifying Your MVAs

## Step 1: List All Activities
Using the worksheet:
- List every role you play at work
- Think in “hats” (manager, coordinator, technician, admin, etc.)
- Include travel and time‑wasting as activities (they matter for awareness)

---

## Step 2: Assign Value
For each activity:
- Estimate annual salary for someone doing that role
- Divide by **2,080 hours** (average work year)
- This gives a rough hourly value

Precision isn’t necessary — clarity is.

---

## Step 3: Rank the Activities
Order activities from highest to lowest hourly value.

- Rank #1 and #2 = your **MVAs**
- Travel and wasted time rank last (value = $0)

These rankings guide future time decisions.

---

# Budgeting Time for Your Most Valuable Activities

## What Is a Time Budget?
A time budget is:
- A template of an *ideal* week
- Not a rigid schedule
- A decision‑making guide

Create a separate calendar called **Time Budget**.

---

## Start with 40% for MVAs
A strong starting point is:
- **40% of total work hours** devoted to MVAs

Example:
- 40‑hour workweek → 16 hours for MVAs
- Two MVAs → ~8 hours each

Place these blocks where interruptions are least likely.

---

## Budget LVAs Too (Intentionally)
Optionally assign limited time to LVAs.

This helps:
- Contain low‑value work
- Prevent it from expanding into prime hours

What you budget tends to stay controlled.

---

# Using Your Time Budget in Daily Decisions

## Scheduling an MVA Task
When processing a task:
- Identify it as an MVA
- Estimate duration (>15 minutes → calendar)
- Check your **time budget**
- Schedule during an MVA block, not wherever there’s space

---

## Protecting MVA Time from LVAs
You’ll often receive requests that conflict with MVA blocks.

Instead of automatic yes:
- Propose alternate times
- Suggest later dates
- Ask clarifying questions

Saying no often means *not now*.

---

## Let the Budget Evolve
Your first time budget is a draft.

Revisit it:
- Every 2–3 months
- Adjust based on reality
- Strengthen what works

The goal is protection, not rigidity.

---

## Reflection Questions
- How many hours do I truly work each week?
- Where does work spill into my personal time?
- What are my two most valuable activities?
- What blocks of time must be protected more fiercely?

---

## Key Takeaway
Lasting productivity requires intentional boundaries and value‑based decisions.

By:
- Separating work time from you time
- Establishing a finish line
- Identifying MVAs
- Budgeting time around value, not urgency

You increase impact, reclaim balance, and make your time work *for* you — not against you.
