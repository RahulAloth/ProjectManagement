## Overview

**Project deliverables** are the results a project produces.  
To determine whether deliverables meet expectations, project managers define **success criteria**, which are measurable ways to evaluate those results.

Deliverables and success criteria together:
- Define project scope
- Guide progress tracking
- Enable objective acceptance of results

---

## What Are Project Deliverables?

Project deliverables are **outputs produced by the project**.

They can be:
- **Tangible**
  - Buildings
  - Products
  - Systems
  - Services
- **Intangible or abstract**
  - Improved performance
  - Reduced error rates
  - Increased satisfaction

Deliverables clarify:
- What *is included* in the project
- What *is not included*

This clarity is essential for managing scope and expectations.

---

## Types of Project Deliverables

---

## 1. End Deliverables

**End deliverables** are the final results delivered at the conclusion of the project.

### Characteristics
- Provided at project completion
- Typically approved by the project customer
- Represent fulfillment of the project goal

### Example: Hospital Scheduling Project
- *“New scheduling system and processes launched”*

---

## 2. Intermediate Deliverables

**Intermediate deliverables** are results produced **during** the project.

### Characteristics
- Occur throughout the project lifecycle
- Support progress toward end deliverables
- May or may not require customer approval

### Examples
- Signed contract with the scheduling system vendor
- Completion of initial system testing
- Finalized training materials draft

The customer does not always review or approve intermediate deliverables.

---

## Using Deliverables to Track Progress

A best practice is to define deliverables that can be completed **between status reports**.

This allows you to:
- Measure progress objectively
- Report accomplishments clearly
- Avoid vague status updates

Progress is assessed by **what has been delivered**, not just time spent.

---

## What Are Success Criteria?

**Success criteria** define **how you know a deliverable is acceptable**.

They are:
- Measurements or conditions
- Used to evaluate whether deliverables meet expectations
- Essential for objective acceptance

Without success criteria, approval becomes subjective and inconsistent.

---

## Types of Success Criteria

---

## Clearly Measurable Criteria

Some success criteria are straightforward.

Examples:
- Signed vendor contract
- Certificate of occupancy for a building
- System installed and operational

These criteria are easy to verify.

---

## Subjective Criteria (Made Measurable)

Other criteria are more subjective and require careful definition.

Best practice:
- Translate subjective outcomes into **quantifiable measures**

### Example: Hospital Scheduling Project
- Increased customer satisfaction defined as:
  - *At least a 4 out of 5 rating on customer surveys*

Clear metrics remove ambiguity.

---

## Writing Effective Success Criteria

Effective success criteria should be:
- **Clear**: Easy to understand
- **Quantifiable**: Measurable numerically or with defined standards
- **Unambiguous**: No room for interpretation

Well‑written success criteria support:
- Fair evaluation
- Faster approvals
- Reduced disputes

---

## Relationship Between Deliverables and Success Criteria

- **Deliverables** describe *what* the project produces
- **Success criteria** describe *how you determine if those deliverables are acceptable*

Both are required to:
- Define project scope
- Measure progress
- Confirm project completion

---

## Practice Exercise

For practice:
1. Identify **end deliverables** for the hospital scheduling project
2. Identify **intermediate deliverables**
3. Define **clear and quantifiable success criteria** for each deliverable

This exercise reinforces how deliverables and success criteria work together.

---

## Key Takeaway

Deliverables define what the project delivers.  
Success criteria define how success is measured.

By clearly identifying both, project managers:
- Clarify scope
- Track meaningful progress
- Ensure stakeholder satisfaction
- Enable objective project acceptance

Projects succeed when results are clearly defined and measurably achieved.

# Identify Project Assumptions and Risks

## Overview

Another key aspect of defining a project during initiation is identifying **project assumptions** and **project risks**.

Both assumptions and risks exist because:
- You do not yet have complete information
- The future is uncertain

Identifying them early helps stakeholders make informed decisions and reduces surprises later in the project.

---

## Project Assumptions

### What Are Assumptions?

Project assumptions are **things you accept as true so you can move forward**, even though you cannot yet confirm them.

During initiation:
- Complete information is rarely available
- Assumptions allow planning to begin
- Assumptions can be revisited and updated as more information becomes clear

---

## Example: Hospital Scheduling Project

You may not know:
- How much system customization will be required
- Which vendor will be selected
- How many internal resources will be needed

### Example Assumption
- The new scheduling system will require **minimal customization**

From this assumption, you might estimate:
- Three internal IT resources
- One month of effort

If the assumption changes later, estimates must be updated.

---

## Why Assumptions Can Be Risky

Assumptions become dangerous when:
- People are not aware they are making them
- Different stakeholders make **different assumptions**
- Assumptions are not communicated or documented

### Example of Misaligned Assumptions

- You assume the system vendor will load data into the database
- The vendor assumes your internal IT team will handle data loading

If this assumption is not identified:
- Work may be missed
- Deadlines can slip
- Responsibility disputes can arise

---

## Best Practices for Managing Assumptions

To manage assumptions effectively:
- Bring assumptions **out into the open**
- Document them explicitly
- Review them with stakeholders
- Revisit and update them as the project evolves

Ask stakeholders:
- What do you expect will happen?
- What do you envision the final solution to look like?
- Who do you believe is responsible for specific activities?

Do not hesitate to ask questions more than once.

---

## Project Risks

### What Is a Risk?

A **risk** is a possible future event or situation that:
- Is uncertain
- Could affect the project positively or negatively

What makes something a risk is **uncertainty**.

---

## Why Identify Risks Early?

Early in the project:
- Management must decide whether investing in the project makes sense
- Risks inform that decision

If:
- Many risks are identified
- Several are severe or unacceptable

Then management may decide:
- Not to proceed with the project
- To delay it
- To pursue a different initiative instead

---

## Risks Can Be Positive or Negative

- **Negative risks** threaten schedule, cost, scope, or quality
- **Positive risks** represent opportunities that could benefit the project

Both should be identified and understood.

---

## Relationship Between Assumptions and Risks

- Assumptions often **mask risks**
- If an assumption turns out to be incorrect, it becomes a risk event

Identifying assumptions helps uncover hidden risks.

---

## Why Assumptions and Risks Must Be Identified Up Front

Assumptions and risks are manageable when:
- They are identified early
- They are documented
- Stakeholders are aware of them

They become dangerous when:
- They remain hidden
- They differ between stakeholders
- They are discovered too late

---

## Practice Exercise

For practice:
- Identify several assumptions for the hospital scheduling project
- Identify potential risks that could affect:
  - Schedule
  - Budget
  - Resources
  - Technology
  - Stakeholder support

This exercise helps sharpen your ability to anticipate uncertainty.

---

## Key Takeaway

Uncertainty is unavoidable in projects.

By identifying:
- **Assumptions** — what you believe to be true for now
- **Risks** — what might happen and affect the project

you give stakeholders the visibility they need to:
- Make informed decisions
- Reduce surprises
- Improve the chances of project success

Projects succeed not by avoiding uncertainty, but by **managing it deliberately**.

## Overview

After completing the work of defining the project, it is time to **put the project definition in writing**.  
This is done through the **project scope statement**.

Project scope represents the **culmination of all initiation activities**.  
It defines the project’s **boundaries** by stating clearly:
- What is **included** in the project
- What is **not included** in the project

---

## What Is Project Scope?

Project scope describes:
- The work the project will perform
- The results the project will deliver

Equally important, it also defines:
- What the project **will not** do

Without agreed‑upon scope boundaries, projects are vulnerable to confusion and uncontrolled changes.

---

## Why the Scope Must Be Written Down

### 1. Prevent Scope Creep

**Scope creep** occurs when additional work is added without formal approval.

Examples of scope creep:
- “Can you just add this one small thing?”
- “We forgot to ask for this—can you include it?”
- “This shouldn’t take long, right?”

If changes are accepted casually and bypass **change management**, the project can:
- Expand beyond original intent
- Exceed budget
- Miss deadlines

---

### 2. Set Clear Expectations

A written scope statement:
- Documents what stakeholders agreed to at project start
- Provides a reference point for resolving misunderstandings

If a stakeholder later says:
> “I thought the project included X, Y, and Z.”

You can:
- Refer back to the scope statement
- Show what is **out of scope**
- Use change management if new work is desired

---

## What Goes Into a Scope Statement

The scope statement pulls together everything defined during project initiation, including:

- Project goal
- Project objectives
- Deliverables
- Success criteria
- Assumptions
- Risks
- Constraints

Together, these elements create a **clear and complete picture** of what the project will deliver.

---

## In‑Scope and Out‑of‑Scope Sections

A good scope statement always includes:
- **In‑Scope** items (what the project includes)
- **Out‑of‑Scope** items (what the project excludes)

---

## Example: Hospital Scheduling Project

### In Scope

- Implement a new hospital scheduling system
- Improve scheduling processes
- Enable efficient coordination of:
  - Staff
  - Equipment
  - Facilities

---

### Out of Scope

- Updates to the system used to assign staff to work shifts
- Scheduling of resident rooms in:
  - The hospital wing
  - The rehabilitation wing

Clearly documenting these exclusions avoids confusion and unrealistic expectations.

---

## Scope Control and Project Success

Keeping scope under control is one of the **most important responsibilities** of a project manager.

A written scope statement:
- Guides planning and execution
- Supports decision‑making
- Enables effective change management
- Reduces conflict with stakeholders

Projects are far more likely to succeed when scope boundaries are explicit and agreed upon.

---

## Practice Exercise

To apply these concepts:
- Use the **scope statement template** in the Exercise Files folder
- Create a project scope statement for the **hospital scheduling project**
- Clearly define:
  - In‑scope work
  - Out‑of‑scope work

---

## Key Takeaway

The project scope statement formalizes what the project is—and what it is not.

By documenting scope clearly and early, project managers:
- Prevent uncontrolled changes
- Align stakeholder expectations
- Protect schedule and budget
- Improve overall project success

A well‑defined scope is a cornerstone of effective project management.

# A Project Charter Checklist

## Overview

A **project charter** formally announces that a project is starting.  
It communicates essential information about the project and defines the **project manager’s role, responsibilities, and authority**.

Not every project needs every possible charter section.  
For smaller projects, a summarized version is often sufficient.  
Use judgment to include only what is necessary to clearly convey:
- Project scope
- Resourcing
- Risks
- Authority and decision rights

---

## Comprehensive Project Charter Checklist

The following list represents **everything that might be included** in a project charter.  
Use the level of detail appropriate for your project before planning begins.

---

## Executive Summary

- Overview of project goals
- High‑level approach
- Key personnel required
- Description of expected business value

---

## Scope Definition

### In Scope
- What the project will produce
- Deliverables or capabilities included

### Out of Scope
- Objectives or business areas the project will not address

---

## Project Manager and Sponsor Roles

### Project Manager
- Responsibilities
- Authority and delegation levels
- Spending limits
- Types of changes the project manager may approve
- Constraints on change approval
- Responsibility for managing project personnel performance
- Process for accessing contingency funds

### Sponsor
- Responsibilities
- Meetings the sponsor will attend
- Process for accessing and allocating management reserve
- Decisions reserved for the sponsor
- Information required to support sponsor decision‑making

---

## Project Methodology

- Waterfall, Agile, or Hybrid
- Exceptions or deviations from organizational standards

### Project Control Deliverables
- Control deliverables not required (e.g., procurement plan if no purchasing is needed)
- Expanded control deliverables when justified  
  (e.g., enhanced risk plan for high‑impact risk projects)

---

## High‑Level Communication Plan

- Who originates communications
- What information is communicated
- Communication timing and frequency
- Where project data is stored and sourced

---

## Assumptions

- Current assumptions
- Approach for validating assumptions

**Example:**  
Assumption: All financial data is processed through a central accounting system.  
Validation approach: Confirm with finance team members that spreadsheets are not used.

---

## Constraints

### Cost
- Maximum project cost (e.g., €300,000)

### Timeframe
- Required completion date (e.g., 1 June)

### Scope
- Mandatory deliverables or capabilities

### Must‑Haves
- Mandatory features (e.g., changes required to retain market share)

### Nice‑to‑Haves
- Optional features that add value but are not essential

### Regulatory Requirements
- Laws or regulations the project must comply with

---

## Resources Required

- Key skills
- Required roles or individuals
- Level of commitment needed for project success

---

## High‑Level Risks

- Potential events or situations
- Risks that could substantially impact the project

---

## Project Success Criteria

- How success will be measured  
  (e.g., number of completed components per month)
- Definition of acceptable performance  

**Example:**  
A 20% increase in components produced per month compared to baseline.

---

## Typical (Simplified) Project Charter Contents

For many projects, a charter includes fewer items documented with less detail:

- Scope definition  
  - In scope  
  - Out of scope
- Project manager responsibilities
- Project methodology
- Assumptions
- Constraints
- Resources required
- High‑level risks
- Project success criteria

---

## Purpose of the Project Charter

The project charter:
- Officially authorizes the project
- Communicates essential project information
- Establishes authority and accountability
- Aligns stakeholders before planning begins

---

## Key Takeaway

A project charter does not need to be complex to be effective.  
Its purpose is clarity—not volume.

A well‑structured charter:
- Sets expectations early
- Defines authority and boundaries
- Reduces misunderstandings
- Provides a solid foundation for successful project planning

# Create a Project Charter

## Overview

Once the **project definition is complete** and the **scope statement is ready**, the next step is to obtain approval to move into **project planning**.

At this point, you create a **project charter**.  
The project charter:
- Officially authorizes the project
- Publicizes the project across the organization
- Grants authority to the project manager

Creating the charter is the **final step in project initiation**.

---

## Purpose of the Project Charter

The purpose of defining a project is to provide the customer or management team with enough information to make an **approval decision**.

The project charter:
- Communicates what the project is about
- Explains why it should be pursued
- Clarifies who is responsible and authorized to act

---

## Project Review and Approval Outcomes

The project review process varies by organization, but typically the customer or management team compares the project information to **acceptance criteria** aligned with organizational goals.

There are three possible outcomes:

1. **Approved**  
   - The project may proceed to planning
2. **Denied**  
   - The project will not move forward
3. **Returned for rework**  
   - Additional clarification or changes are required

---

## When the Project Is Approved

Once the project is approved:
- A project charter is created
- The charter is distributed to relevant stakeholders
- The project is formally authorized

This authorization signals that the project may begin planning.

---

## Typical Contents of a Project Charter

A project charter contains **high‑level information**, not detailed plans.  
These elements will be expanded during project planning.

---

### Project Information

- Project name
- Project purpose
- Brief summary of:
  - Project goal
  - Key objectives

---

### High‑Level Project Description

May include:
- High‑level success criteria
- Major requirements
- Summary of project scope
- Key risks
- Key assumptions
- Key constraints

This section describes the project at a level sufficient for executive understanding.

---

### Schedule, Cost, and Stakeholders

- High‑level milestone schedule
- Rough cost estimate
- Identified stakeholders

These are **initial estimates**, not final plans.

---

### Project Manager Information

The charter documents:
- Project manager name
- Project manager responsibilities
- Description of the work the project manager performs

---

### Project Manager Authority

A critical part of the charter is defining **project manager authority**, including:
- Scope of decision‑making authority
- Ability to request or assign resources
- Authority to approve work
- Authority to sign contracts (if applicable)

This clarification is essential because:
- Project managers do not have permanent organizational authority
- Their authority exists only for the duration of the project
- Their authority applies only to the project itself

---

### Sponsor Authorization

The charter includes a **formal declaration of sponsor support**.

This declaration:
- Confirms the sponsor’s commitment
- Authorizes the project manager to act
- Serves as a “power of attorney” for the project

The sponsor’s approval gives the project manager legitimacy and backing.

---

## Why Project Manager Authority Must Be Explicit

Unlike functional managers:
- Project managers do not control permanent resources
- Their authority is temporary and project‑specific

Documenting authority in the charter ensures:
- Stakeholders understand decision rights
- Conflicts are minimized
- The project manager can act effectively

---

## Distributing the Project Charter

Once finalized:
- The sponsor distributes the project charter
- Everyone affected by or involved in the project receives it

This ensures:
- Visibility
- Alignment
- Understanding of roles and authority

---

## Transition to Planning

After the charter is distributed:
- The project is officially authorized
- The project manager’s authority is understood
- The project is ready to move into **planning**

---

## Key Takeaway

The project charter:
- Formally starts the project
- Establishes authority and accountability
- Aligns expectations before planning begins

A clear and approved charter sets the foundation for effective project planning and execution.
