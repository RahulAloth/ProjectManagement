# Use Your Strengths to Help the Team

## Knowing Your Strengths Creates Responsibility
Once you become aware of what you are good at, you also take on a responsibility: **to use those strengths for the benefit of the team**.

Sometimes people hold back not because they can’t contribute, but because of:
- Modesty (“I don’t want to push myself forward”)
- Habit (“That’s not normally my role”)
- Comfort (“I’ll just do what I always do”)

Great team players don’t hide their strengths — they offer them.

---

## Volunteer Where You Can Add Value
A simple starting point is to ask:
- What challenges is the team facing right now?
- Which of my strengths could help with those challenges?

Instead of waiting to be asked, step forward when you know you can help. Volunteering shows awareness, commitment, and confidence — not ego.

---

## Look at It from the Other Direction
Another useful question is:
> *What am I naturally good at — and when can I apply it?*

Sometimes teams struggle not because help is unavailable, but because capable people don’t realize their skills are relevant or needed.

---

## If You’re Unsure About Your Strengths
If identifying your strengths feels difficult, try two practical approaches:

### 1. Ask Others
Ask colleagues questions such as:
- “What do you think I’m best at contributing?”
- “When do I add the most value to the team?”

Their responses may surprise you — positively or instructively.

---

### 2. Use a Role Framework
Review a list of common team contributions and see which resonate with you, such as:
- Organizing people or information
- Driving progress and momentum
- Networking and gathering ideas
- Generating creative solutions
- Questioning plans and spotting risks
- Designing systems or processes
- Ensuring accuracy and detail

You may discover strengths you’ve been under‑using or ignoring.

---

## Strengths You Don’t Always Use
Some strengths remain unused because:
- They make you slightly uncomfortable
- They create mild anxiety
- You don’t *enjoy* them, even though you’re good at them
- You don’t get many chances to practice them

Discomfort does not mean inability. Often, it signals growth potential.

---

## Strengths You Don’t Yet Fully Own
You may also have abilities that:
- You don’t think of as strengths
- You haven’t practiced recently
- Haven’t been called upon yet

With a little experimentation, these can become reliable contributions.

---

## Choose One Way to Step Up
Rather than trying to change everything at once, do one simple thing:
- Identify a role or task you could contribute more to
- Volunteer for it consciously
- Observe the impact on the team — and on your confidence

Small steps make growth sustainable.

---

## Reflection Questions
Spend a few minutes thinking about:
- What am I good at that the team needs?
- Where could I help more than I currently do?
- Which strengths do I tend to keep hidden?
- What is one additional contribution I could volunteer for next?

Awareness creates opportunity.

---

## Key Takeaway
Great teams benefit when individuals **intentionally use what they’re good at**.

By:
- Recognizing your strengths
- Offering them without being asked
- Expanding beyond your comfort zone

You increase both your value to the team and your own satisfaction in the work you do.

# Explore Your Job, Your Comfort Zone, and Your Potential

## Self‑Understanding Comes from Action, Not Just Reflection
Understanding yourself isn’t something you achieve once and then keep forever. It develops over time, through **feedback and experimentation**.

One effective way to learn more about yourself is to deliberately try:
- Different roles within a team
- Tasks you don’t usually volunteer for
- Responsibilities that stretch you beyond what feels safe or familiar

Growth often comes from doing things you’re unsure about, not only from doing what you already do well.

---

## Step Outside Your Comfort Zone — Carefully
Your comfort zone is the area where tasks feel familiar and manageable. Working there is pleasant, but staying there too long can lead to:
- Stagnation
- Reduced motivation
- Missed potential

Stepping outside your comfort zone doesn’t mean reckless risk. It means **intentional stretching** — testing yourself in manageable ways and learning from the experience.

You may discover abilities you didn’t realize you had.

---

## A Simple Three‑Number Self‑Assessment
Here’s a practical exercise to help you assess where you are right now. Score each area from 1 to 10.

### 1. How Demanding Is Your Job?
- 10 = Constantly challenging and stretching
- 3 = Easy to the point of boredom

This reflects how much your role *requires* of you.

---

### 2. How Strong Is Your Current Performance?
- 10 = Performing at the top of your ability
- 3 or lower = At risk and struggling

This reflects how well you’re currently delivering.

---

### 3. How Large Is Your Comfort Zone?
- 10 = Many tasks feel easy and safe
- 3 = Most tasks feel uncomfortable or anxiety‑provoking

This reflects where you feel confident, not necessarily where you perform best.

---

## Interpreting the Numbers
Looking at the three scores together can be revealing.

### Example A: Cruising
- Job demand: 5
- Performance: 5
- Comfort zone: 8

This suggests you’re operating well within what feels easy. That may be comfortable — but it might also mean you could push yourself more.

---

### Example B: Stretching and Growing
- Job demand: 8
- Performance: 8
- Comfort zone: 5

This shows you’re consistently operating beyond what feels comfortable — and succeeding. This is often where the most learning happens, even if it doesn’t always feel pleasant.

---

## There Is No “Correct” Answer
The goal isn’t to maximize discomfort — it’s to balance:
- Performance
- Learning
- Well‑being

Ask yourself:
- Am I pushing myself enough to grow?
- Am I pushing myself too hard and risking burnout?
- Where am I learning new skills?
- Where am I coasting?

---

## Identify the Stretch Points in Your Role
Look honestly at your work:
- Which parts of your job challenge you?
- Which parts feel routine?
- Are there responsibilities that make you slightly nervous — and therefore offer growth?

Stretching areas are often uncomfortable but valuable.

---

## Notice Untapped Potential
There may be abilities you haven’t explored yet because:
- You’ve never been asked
- You’ve avoided them in the past
- They feel intimidating
- You don’t see yourself “that way”

Potential almost always sits **just beyond** current comfort zones.

---

## Reframe Discomfort as Development
Tasks that push you outside your comfort zone can feel frustrating or tiring. Try reframing them as:
- Evidence that you’re learning
- Signals of growth
- Investments in future capability

What feels difficult today often becomes tomorrow’s strength.

---

## Reflection Questions
Take time to consider:
- Are parts of my job stretching me right now?
- Am I developing for the future or just delivering for today?
- Where could I experiment with new roles or skills?
- Which potential am I not currently using?

Even small experiments can produce valuable insight.

---

## Key Takeaway
Self‑development doesn’t happen automatically — it requires curiosity and courage.

By:
- Trying unfamiliar tasks
- Stepping slightly beyond comfort
- Reflecting honestly on challenge and performance

You increase your future capability — not just for yourself, but for every team you work with.

# Three Ways to Approach Your Weaknesses

## Why Weakness Awareness Matters
Understanding your weaknesses is just as important — and often more important — than knowing your strengths.

When both you *and* your teammates are aware of:
- Your limitations
- Your tendencies under pressure
- The situations where you struggle

The team can plan more realistically and perform more effectively.

Weaknesses only cause problems when they are hidden or denied.

---

## Weaknesses Are Not Personal Failures
Everyone has areas where they are less effective:
- Struggling with detail
- Losing patience when tired
- Being overly optimistic
- Disliking networking or small talk
- Avoiding structure or planning

Acknowledging these openly reduces friction and allows the team to work around them intelligently.

---

## Option 1: Avoid Situations That Expose the Weakness
The simplest approach is avoidance.

If you know a task heavily depends on a skill you lack, it may be sensible to:
- Step away from that responsibility
- Delegate it to someone better suited
- Avoid putting yourself in situations where failure is likely

This can be effective — but only if avoidance doesn’t harm the team or limit your growth.

---

## Option 2: Get Coverage from the Team
This is often the **most powerful and healthiest option**.

Teams exist precisely because:
- Different people enjoy different tasks
- Strengths and weaknesses can complement each other

If you dislike detail, someone else may love it.  
If you avoid social interaction, another teammate may thrive on it.

In return, you can offer help in areas *they* avoid but *you* find easy.

This mutual coverage is the essence of teamwork.

---

## Option 3: Develop the Weakness
The third option is deliberate self‑development.

Some people argue that trying to improve weaknesses is pointless — that you’ll never excel without natural talent. While it’s true you may never be world‑class, you can often become **good enough** to function confidently.

The real question is:
> *Do I need to develop this skill — or can the team cover it better?*

---

## When Development Is Worth It
Self‑development is beneficial when:
- The weakness regularly limits your contribution
- It affects reliability or trust
- It restricts your future opportunities
- It keeps you stuck in a narrow role

Often, the skills we avoid most are the ones that unlock the greatest growth.

---

## Don’t Let Comfort Zones Decide for You
Sometimes we avoid developing weaknesses because:
- They feel uncomfortable
- They make us anxious
- We’ve labelled them as “not me”

But until you try, you don’t know how far you could improve.

Even partial improvement can:
- Increase confidence
- Reduce dependency
- Expand your usefulness to the team

---

## Combining the Three Options
Most weaknesses don’t need a single solution.

You might:
- Avoid a task occasionally
- Rely on colleagues in high‑stakes situations
- Practice and improve gradually over time

Flexibility matters more than ideology.

---

## A Practical Reflection Exercise
Create a simple list with three columns:

1. **My main weaknesses**
2. **How this affects the team**
3. **My approach**
   - Avoid
   - Get coverage
   - Develop

If you struggle to identify weaknesses, ask someone you trust — gently and honestly.

---

## Example Insights
- Avoiding all weaknesses leads to stagnation  
- Relying entirely on others can limit independence  
- Developing selectively builds confidence and adaptability  

The strongest team members are not flawless — they are **self‑aware and proactive**.

---

## Key Takeaway
Weaknesses don’t disqualify you from being a great team player.  
Ignoring them does.

By:
- Acknowledging your limitations
- Choosing when to avoid, cover, or develop
- Being honest with yourself and the team

You become more dependable, more adaptable, and more valuable — both now and in the future.
